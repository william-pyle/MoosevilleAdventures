# Section 1 — Executive Summary & Elevator Pitch


This section provides the top-level creative brief for Mooseville Adventures — the single authoritative statement of what this game is, who it is for, and why it deserves to exist. Every team member should be able to recite this section from memory.

## 1.1 Elevator Pitch


The Mooseville Adventures Pitch
Mooseville Adventures is a hybrid cozy-adventure game set 22 years after a magical meteor strike awakened a valley of moose into a fully realized civilization. Playing as a grown child of the town's legendary founders — Melvin and Martha — you tend your home, forge bonds with your community, and venture into an ancient and changing wilderness to unravel the mysteries the Starfall left behind. It is a game about belonging, discovery, and what it means to carry your family's legacy forward.

# Section 2 — Game Vision & Design Pillars


This section defines the creative north star of Mooseville Adventures. Every design decision — from crop timers to quest pacing to UI font choices — must be measured against these pillars. If a feature does not serve at least one pillar, it does not belong in the game.

## 2.1 The Mooseville Promise


The Mooseville Promise
Every hour in Mooseville Adventures should feel like a warm autumn afternoon — productive, peaceful, and full of small moments worth remembering. Whether the player is mending a neighbor's fence, fishing at the crystal lake at golden hour, or pushing through a dark pine forest toward a glowing stone no one has named yet, the game should never feel hostile or punishing. Challenge comes from curiosity, not combat. Progress comes from relationships, not grinding. And every mystery, once solved, should feel earned — because the player found it by caring about the world around them.

## 2.2 Five Named Design Pillars

These five pillars are the binding agreement between all disciplines. They are listed here in full canonical form. When in doubt — return here.

Pillar 1 — "Roots and Routes"
The player has deep roots in Mooseville (family legacy, known neighbors, inherited home) AND open routes into a wilderness that has never been fully mapped. Both matter equally. No session should feel like it only did one or the other. Designers must ensure that every play session offers both a grounding village activity and an invitation to explore — even if the player chooses only one.


Pillar 2 — "Every Moosian Has a Story"
NPCs are not quest dispensers. Every named Moosian in town has a life that continues whether or not the player interacts with them. Relationships deepen through repeated presence, small gifts, shared meals, and remembered conversations — not through dialogue trees. No NPC should ever feel like a vending machine for quests or rewards.


Pillar 3 — "The Valley Is Still Changing"
The Starfall happened 22 years ago, but the valley is not finished. New awakened moose still arrive. The sealed eastern pass still groans with old stone. The Green Spring still glows in ways that science in Mooseville can't fully explain. The player lives on a frontier of understanding — and that frontier expands as they explore. The world must feel alive and in motion, not static and solved.


Pillar 4 — "Cozy Has Consequence"
Village life activities — farming, crafting, cooking, gifting — have real mechanical weight. What the player grows feeds real NPC needs. What they craft unlocks real expedition capabilities. The cozy loop and the adventure loop feed each other; neither is a side dish. Designers must resist treating any village system as decorative. Every system must connect to at least one other system with meaningful consequence.


Pillar 5 — "Inherited Wonder"
Melvin and Martha built something extraordinary. Playing as their child means inheriting that wonder — but also the pressure of living up to it, questioning it, and ultimately adding to it. The player's own name goes into Mooseville's history by the end of the game. The narrative arc must honor this emotional weight; the ending is not a triumph over a villain but a contribution to a legacy.

# Section 3 — Lore & World History


This section is the canonical in-game history of Mooseville. All story content, NPC dialogue, quest text, and world design must be consistent with the lore established here. Contradictions found in later design documents or implementation should be flagged to the Narrative Designer and resolved against this section as the authority.


Canonical Status Notice
This lore is final for v1.0. Any changes to canonical history require written approval from the Game Director and Narrative Designer, with version notes appended to this section. Do not implement lore-altering content without documented authorization.

## 3.1 The Valley Before Names

Long before any Moosian ever spoke, the valley was a closed world of forest, river, marsh, cliff, and meadow. Snowmelt fed clear water through deep ravines. Cedar groves and fern hollows sheltered wildlife through harsh winters. Bears came down in late summer. Deer crossed the lower ridges with the season. Beavers cut channels in the southern wetland. Eagles watched from the stone heights.
Moose moved through all of it as steady giants, shaping marsh paths with their weight and opening glades with their feeding. They did not rule the valley, but they held its rhythms together.
The valley carried signs of a much older visitor. Rusted wire, broken survey stakes, and weather-eaten trail markers remained at the edges. On a rise above one creek bend, an abandoned campsite slept beneath leaves and moss — but no living creature in the valley knew what those remnants meant. These human traces become one of the game's three primary mystery threads.
## 3.2 The Starfall

During a spring thaw, a burning rock crossed the morning sky. It broke over the eastern peaks and struck a high shelf of mountain stone. The impact shattered old rock and sent a landslide roaring down through the migration pass — sealing the most reliable way in and out of the basin under ice, granite, and buried soil.
The same fall scattered sky-stone shards into a small spring below. The fragments settled into the springbed and slowly bled a strange influence into the water, night after night. The stream began to carry a faint green glow after dusk. At first, the animals only knew the water tasted different.
## 3.3 The Green Spring and the Awakening

The change came in stages, across months of gradual transformation:
- Stage 1 — Physical Shifts: Stronger hind legs, more dexterous forelimbs, hooves that could grip tools and cloth.
- Stage 2 — Mental Expansion: Sharper memory, deeper curiosity, distinct personality emerging in each individual animal.
- Stage 3 — Speech: Rough at first, but unmistakable. Individual sounds becoming consistent, then shared, then structural.
- Stage 4 — Culture: Symbols, rules, rituals, and shared purpose. The valley had birthed a people.
The moose nearest the spring changed earliest and most deeply. Some said the sky-stone carried a mind from beyond the clouds. Others believed the valley already held a sleeping force that the Starfall merely opened. Whatever the truth, this philosophical tension persists in Mooseville culture to the present day — and the player will encounter both perspectives in NPC dialogue.
## 3.4 The First Gathering and the Rise of Melvin & Martha

The first awakened moose gathered at dusk near the glowing spring, testing sounds, pointing at water, tracks, trees, and stars — building the first shared language. Among those who held the center were Melvin and Martha, a mated wild pair long before awakening.
They were not followed because they claimed authority. They were followed because, even before language, they were the ones who watched over others, lingered beside the weak, and took less than they needed. When thought and speech came, those instincts became leadership. This is the origin of Melvin and Martha's moral authority in Mooseville — and the emotional weight the player inherits.
## 3.5 The Sacred Documents

Seeking answers, the new people found the old campsite above the stream. Inside a half-buried metal trunk were books, magazines, field guides, and printed manuals. The words were unreadable at first, but the pictures were clear: buildings, kitchens, tools, gardens, roads, clothing, workshops, and families gathered in common space.
The awakened moose called these the Sacred Documents — they preserved memory, offered instruction, and proved that meaningful community could be learned and built. The Sacred Documents are a game object the player interacts with directly through Archive Quests. Reconstructing document pages is a primary late-game activity tied to the human remnant mystery thread.
## 3.6 The Founding of Mooseville

The early settlement began as practical shelter near the green stream: drying racks, storage pits, shared fire circles. Over time, a name emerged — "Mooseville" — spoken first by Melvin and Martha over a village image in the Sacred Documents, then repeated and ratified by all.
First public structures: a gathering hall, a protected growing circle for seedlings, an archive shelter for the Sacred Documents, water steps for irrigation, and a high lookout for weather and danger.
Core customs established at founding:
- Food shared before stored
- Young named in public
- Clean water never wasted
- One central fire kept burning each night
Around Melvin and Martha formed the First Circle — eight specialists who gave the town its complete shape:

## 3.7 Culture of Adaptation

Mooseville learned from the Sacred Documents but never tried to become human. Farming methods were adapted to hoof-friendly rows. Clothing was redesigned for broad shoulders, dense neck fur, and antler spread. Antler and mane houses emerged as a distinct architectural form. Carved wooden coins called mucks became local currency for non-essential trade.
Two laws remained inviolable from the first season of founding, and have never been repealed:

The Two Inviolable Laws of Mooseville
I. Moose Water is free.
II. Basic food is free.
These two laws are mechanically enforced in gameplay. The player cannot starve. The player cannot be denied Moose Water. These are not design limitations — they are a deliberate expression of Mooseville's founding values embedded in the game's systems. See Section 12.2 for gameplay implementation details.

## 3.8 The Fading Years (Year 4–5 Post-Starfall)

Roughly four to five years after founding, crisis struck: some citizens began to regress — losing speech clarity, collapsing toward four-hoof movement, losing higher memory. Simultaneously, the spring's glow grew faint. Investigation by Harold Winslow's early water studies revealed the cause: a second rockslide had diverted the green stream away from Mooseville channels. The enriched water now ran to the far side of the valley, away from the population it had awakened.
The Fading Years are Mooseville's most psychologically significant historical trauma. They are referenced in NPC dialogue, Archive records, and the player's own personal family history. Melvin's response to this crisis defines his character — and shapes the player character's legacy.
## 3.9 The Green Water Recovery

Melvin led a recovery team through debris, unstable slopes, and dense timber to the diverted source. They returned with casks of green water, confirmed that controlled dosing restored affected citizens, then mounted a larger effort. The result was Mooseville's local Enhancement Works: a civic station where clean water passes through secured shard chambers and takes on the same beneficial trace as the old spring.
From that era forward, Moose Water (enhanced) and regular water are separate, both protected as public trust. The Enhancement Works remains a central hub in the modern town, and its maintenance becomes a gameplay-critical concern in Act 1.
## 3.10 The Far Side Arrivals and the Age of Invitation

Months after the recovery works stabilized, unknown awakened moose arrived from the opposite side of the valley — changed briefly by diverted green water, now regressing, exhausted, and desperate. Mooseville housed and dosed them. That moment transformed Mooseville forever: it was no longer just the town that survived the Starfall. It became the town that shared survival.
In subsequent years, valley search teams were organized — not for conquest, but for contact, aid, and consent. Teams sought wild moose beyond known routes, offered Moose Water, and gave each recovered individual a clear choice: join or return to the wild. No one was forced. No one was owned. Mooseville grew district by district. This history directly shapes the New Arrival Moose mechanic in gameplay (see Section 10.6).
## 3.11 The Unanswered Questions — Active Mystery Threads


The Three Mystery Threads (Active at Game Start)
These three threads form the backbone of the game's main narrative arc. All three must be present, trackable, and emotionally satisfying to resolve by the end of Act 3. No mystery thread may be resolved by combat or confrontation — each must be solved through discovery, relationship, and earned understanding.
Thread 1 — The Starfall Source: What exactly fell from the sky? Was the sky-stone natural? Intentional? Is there more of it beyond the valley's known geography?
Thread 2 — The Sealed Eastern Pass: The meteor's landslide sealed the migration route 22 years ago. No one has ever crossed to the other side. What is over there? Who might be?
Thread 3 — The Human Remnants: The abandoned campsite, the Sacred Documents, the rusted survey stakes — who were these beings? Did they know what the valley was? Were they here intentionally?

# Section 4 — Setting: The Valley & Mooseville


This section establishes the physical world of Mooseville Adventures in enough detail that Environment Artists, Level Designers, and World Builders can begin blocking out the valley with accurate proportions, biome relationships, and thematic intent.

## 4.1 World Overview

The valley is a mountain-ringed basin roughly 12km × 8km in playable area. It contains multiple distinct biomes, connected by trails, river fords, and natural landmarks. The world is hand-crafted (not procedural) — every landmark, path, and discovery point is intentionally placed. Time of day and seasons cycle in real time. The world should feel curated and purposeful, not algorithmically assembled.
The valley's geography creates natural progression pacing: the town center is immediately accessible, near biomes open early, and far biomes require either Trail Knack upgrades, story progression gates, or seasonal access — ensuring that discovery always feels earned rather than arbitrary.
## 4.2 Mooseville Town — Hub Area

The central hub. Population approximately 80–120 Moosians by game start. The town has grown organically from the original settlement near the Green Spring, spreading outward in rings of increasing specialization. Each district has a distinct visual identity while sharing the core Mooseville aesthetic: warm wood construction, hanging lanterns, antler-shaped architectural details, and emerald garden plots along every path.

# Section 5 — Story & Narrative


This section defines the player's narrative position, the emotional arc of the full game, and the tone that must be maintained across all written content including quest text, journal entries, NPC dialogue, and UI copy. The Narrative Designer is the authority on all content in this section.

## 5.1 The Player's Position

The player character is a grown child of Melvin and Martha, approximately 22 years old at game start — one year for each year since the Starfall. They have grown up in the most storied household in Mooseville: watching their parents build a civilization from nothing, living with the weight of a legendary legacy, and wondering what it means to contribute something of their own.
Melvin and Martha are now respected elders in their early-to-mid life (moose aging slower after awakening). They are not absent — they are present, loving, and proud. But they are ready to step back. The player character's coming-of-age — not their parents' heroism — is the emotional spine of the game.
## 5.2 Opening Sequence

The game begins with the player character returning to Mooseville after a short period living in a far-valley settlement. This framing device establishes that they have briefly left, giving the player a narrative reason to re-learn the town alongside the game's tutorial. Melvin and Martha have gifted them the family's original homestead near the Green Spring — a fixer-upper, full of memories and meaning. The player must make it their own.

Opening Beat — Scripted Sequence
The player arrives in Mooseville at golden hour. Martha hands them the key to the old homestead. Melvin — standing in the doorway of the home they built together — looks at his child and says:
"Everything we built, we built because we didn't look away. Don't look away either."
This line is fully voiced. It plays once. It is not repeated anywhere in the game. It sets the emotional register for everything that follows.

## 5.3 Three-Act Story Structure

### Act 1 — "Learning the Town" (Hours 1–8 approx.)
The player re-integrates into Mooseville life. The act is structured as a warm re-introduction to every district, every key NPC, and the core gameplay loops. The mystery threads are introduced subtly — not as missions, but as curiosities the player stumbles across naturally.
- Player restores the homestead, meets the current NPC generation (children and grandchildren of First Circle members, plus recent new arrivals)
- Encounters the first mystery clue: a journal page from the old trunk that Melvin kept private — found tucked inside the homestead wall during renovation
- Discovers the Enhancement Works are weakening slightly — the sky-stone shards are degrading after 22 years of continuous use
- Act 1 Climax: A small but unmistakable tremor reopens a crack in the direction of the sealed eastern pass. The ground shakes. The town gathers at the Central Fire. Melvin says nothing — but the player catches him looking northeast for a very long time.
### Act 2 — "The Valley Opens" (Hours 8–25 approx.)
The widest act. The player systematically explores all wilderness biomes, deepens relationships with Tier 2 NPCs, and begins to piece together the three mystery threads through artifact discovery and NPC story quests.
- Player restores the weakening Enhancement Works by locating a new sky-stone deposit on the High Shelf — a direct consequence of Act 1's instability reveal
- Uncovers human remnant artifacts in the Ancient Ruins that suggest the valley was intentionally surveyed — not accidentally discovered — decades before the Starfall
- Meets new-arrival moose from beyond the valley who carry a disturbing account: the far side has a second sealed pass, and something is glowing on the other side of it
- Act 2 Climax: The sealed eastern pass cracks open enough to enter for the first time. The player stands at the threshold. This is the game's most visually iconic moment.
### Act 3 — "What The Star Left" (Hours 25–40+ approx.)
The player crosses the eastern pass into unknown territory and confronts the full truth of Mooseville's origin. The act resolves all three mystery threads and delivers the game's central emotional question.
- Discovers the full origin of the sky-stone: a larger fragment network, evidence of intentional origin (not a natural meteor), and human survey notes dating decades before Mooseville existed
- Faces the game's central emotional question: Does it matter where the awakening came from, if what grew from it was real?
- Final Act Narrative Choice: The player makes a decision that determines Mooseville's next chapter — keep the pass open and invite connection with other valley communities, share the knowledge of the Starfall's origin with all Moosians, or protect the valley's isolation and let the mystery remain a mystery. All three endings are warm and hopeful. None is the "wrong" choice.
- Melvin and Martha appear for the final scene together. The player character's name is inscribed in the Archive Hall alongside the First Circle — the final reward, permanent and emotional.

# Section 6 — Player Character


This section defines who the player is in Mooseville Adventures, how they are expressed visually and mechanically, and how their abilities grow through gameplay. The player character is not a blank slate — they have a family, a history, and a town that knows them. That specificity is intentional and must be preserved in all character design decisions.

## 6.1 Character Identity

The player character is canonically a child of Melvin and Martha. Their name is chosen freely by the player at game start and used throughout the game by all NPCs. Character creation is warm and unhurried — framed as getting dressed for the homecoming, not filling out a form.

## 6.2 The Knack System — Ability Progression

The player character gains abilities through the Knack System — five Knack Trees, each tied to a design pillar. Knack progression is entirely emergent: it is driven by what the player actually does, not by spending XP points or choosing a class. A player who spends 10 hours farming naturally earns Harvest Knack tiers. A player who explores constantly builds Trail Knack. The system rewards authentic play — not optimization.

Knack System — Design Intent
Knack Trees must never feel like a grind gate. Each tier should unlock something the player wants — not something they need to access content. The game is fully completable without maximizing any Knack Tree. Knacks are rewards for playing the way you love, not requirements to play at all.

# Section 7 — Core Gameplay Loop


This section defines the rhythmic structure of play in Mooseville Adventures — the beats that repeat across every session and every week. The loop is designed to feel natural and self-motivating: players should always end a session knowing exactly what they want to do next, without any artificial pressure to do it now.

## 7.1 The Daily Loop — Cozy Spine

## 7.4 No Fail State


The No Fail State Commitment
Mooseville Adventures has no fail state. The player cannot die. They cannot go broke (basic food and water are always free — see Section 12.2). They cannot permanently miss content. Quests wait patiently. NPCs forgive absence. Time-limited events offer alternative completion paths if missed.
This is not a difficulty oversight — it is a deliberate design choice rooted in the Mooseville Promise. Anxiety-free play is core to this game's identity and audience. Programmers and designers must never implement a game-over condition, a permanent quest lock, or a resource floor of zero without explicit Game Director approval.

# Section 8 — Village Life Systems


This section defines all systems that constitute the "cozy spine" of Mooseville Adventures. These systems must be designed to feel satisfying in isolation AND meaningfully connected to every other system. No village system is decorative — each has consequence for exploration, relationships, and narrative progression.

## 8.1 Farming System

The player owns a personal farm plot adjacent to the homestead. The plot starts small (8 crop slots) and expands through crafted infrastructure (up to 32 slots across 4 expansions). Farm plots are grid-based; each slot holds one crop type at a time.
### Core Farming Actions
- Watering (daily): Standard water from the Town Creek or the homestead well. Required for crop growth. Moose Water optional for enhanced variants.
- Weeding (weekly): Neglected plots reduce yield by 30%. Not mandatory but rewarding.
- Fertilizing (optional): Crafted fertilizer increases yield by 50% for one growth cycle.
- Seasonal Rotation: Crops cannot be planted out of season (with one exception: greenhouse slots function in any season).
### Moose Water Irrigation
Applying Moose Water to a crop slot (costs 1 portion per slot) accelerates growth by 20% and produces the Enhanced Variant of that crop on harvest. Enhanced Variants are the most powerful gifting items in the game and unlock advanced recipes.

### Fishing Mechanic
A simple rhythm-based minigame: a timing bar with a moving indicator; player taps at the right moment to set the hook, then holds within a comfort zone to reel. # Section 9 — Social & Relationship System


The relationship system is the emotional engine of Mooseville Adventures. It must feel human — not gamified. NPCs should feel like neighbors, not progress bars. Every design decision in this system must serve the feeling of genuine friendship, not the feeling of unlocking rewards.

## 9.1 Philosophy

Relationships in Mooseville are not managed through a dialogue menu or gift-spam loop. They develop through accumulated presence and remembered interactions. The game tracks what the player has done with each NPC and reflects it organically in dialogue, availability, and the depth of quests they offer. A player who spends three weeks fishing with Storm Winslow will have a fundamentally different experience of Storm than a player who only completed his assigned quest.
## 9.2 Relationship Mechanics

Each named NPC has a Closeness Level from 1 to 6. Closeness is never displayed as a numerical value to the player — instead, it manifests as changes in how NPCs speak to the player, what they share, and where they are willing to go.

### Closeness Growth Actions
- Gifting — especially cooked meals (highest value), crafted items, and foraged finds the NPC has mentioned wanting
- Showing up — visiting an NPC during their scheduled hours at their usual location, consistently
- Quest completion — completing their personal arc quests
- Event attendance — attending events they host or care about
- Remembered conversations — the player engages with topics the NPC cares about
### Remembered Conversations System
The game logs up to 5 remembered conversation tags per NPC. These are topics the player has discussed with that NPC — a fishing trip, a crop failure, a discovery in the woods. NPCs reference these tags in future dialogue naturally and unprompted. This system is implemented via the SaveGame object and referenced by the NPC Dialogue Graph at conversation start. NPCs who have more remembered conversations with the player speak with noticeably more warmth and specificity.
## 9.3 NPC Schedule System

Every named NPC has a full daily schedule implemented via the DailySchedule Actor Component. NPCs move through their routine across all time-of-day phases whether or not the player is nearby. Players learn NPC schedules through observation, conversation, and community board announcements — not through a map indicator (until Closeness Level 2).
This design creates the feeling of a town that lives independently — one of the most important atmospheric contributions of the social system. Players who pay attention are rewarded with found moments: Chester at his forge at dawn, Melvin and Martha walking to the Green Spring at dusk, Clara working late in the Archive Quarter on rainy nights.

# Section 10 — Exploration & Quest Systems


This section defines how the player moves through the world beyond Mooseville and how they engage with the game's structured content. The guiding principle is that exploration should always feel like discovery, and quests should always feel like stories — never like checklists.

## 10.1 Exploration Philosophy

The wilderness is not a danger zone — it is a wonder zone. There are no enemies. Obstacles are environmental in nature: stamina barriers, locked areas requiring tools or relationship keys, and seasonal access gates. The reward for exploration is discovery, lore, materials, and new NPC encounters. The wilderness should feel like it is waiting for the player — curious, patient, and full of things that will only become meaningful once the player understands the history behind them.

The Exploration Promise
Every biome must contain at least one thing the player cannot fully understand the first time they find it. The valley earns its depth by rewarding return visits — a ruin that made no sense in Act 1 becomes profoundly legible in Act 3. Environment Artists should design with this principle in mind: plant questions before planting answers.

## 10.4 Quest Journal

The Personal Journal is the player's primary interface with quest tracking. It is a hand-drawn, in-character notebook written in the player character's voice — automatically updated after key events, discoveries, and conversations. It is not a task list. It reads like a diary with purpose.
- Active quests are noted with the player character's own emotional reaction to them, not just the objective
- Completed quests are marked with a small hand-sketched illustration of the key moment
- Quest markers are optional — a toggle in settings hides all world-space markers for players who prefer pure exploration
- The Journal is accessed through the main menu interface (the leather-bound journal object), never through a pause-menu list
## 10.5 Wilderness Discovery System

- The world map starts partially blank — filled in through player exploration. Each new biome area entered generates an automatic journal entry with the player character's first impressions.
- Landmark Naming: When the player discovers an unnamed landmark (a waterfall, a ruin, a stone formation, a meadow overlook), they are given the option to name it. Named landmarks appear on the town map permanently. NPCs reference the player's chosen name in dialogue afterward — "Oh, you went out past what you're calling the Antler Falls? Beautiful out there."
- Atlas Reed's map fills in systematically as the player explores — each biome unlocked with the player earns a map section from Atlas (see his NPC profile in Section 13).
## 10.6 New Arrival Moose Encounters

At certain wilderness locations, the player may encounter a recently awakened moose — partially regressed, confused, and in need of help. These encounters are among the game's most emotionally resonant moments and directly mirror Mooseville's founding values of invitation and consent.
### Encounter Arc (Multi-Step)
- Approach carefully — the moose is startled; the player must move slowly (a proximity patience mechanic)
- Communicate gently — a simple choice-based interaction (3 options); choices affect the encounter tone but not the outcome
- Provide Moose Water — costs 1 Moose Water portion; restores the new arrival's clarity and allows fuller communication
- Offer a choice — join Mooseville, or be assisted back to their home territory. The player respects the choice either way.
Each new arrival who joins Mooseville becomes a permanent NPC with their own daily schedule, relationship arc, and unique backstory. Up to 8 new arrivals can join over the course of a full playthrough. The New Arrivals District in Mooseville grows visibly as each arrival settles in.

# Section 12 — Economy & Currency


Mooseville's economy must feel generous, fair, and philosophically aligned with the Two Laws. The player should never feel financially anxious. The economy's purpose is to give meaningful weight to player choices about how to spend surplus — not to create scarcity or pressure.

## 12.1 Mucks

Mucks are carved wooden coins — the only currency in Mooseville. They are beautiful objects in the world: hand-carved discs with the Green Spring glyph on one face and the founding year on the other. They are exchanged only for non-essential goods and services. Their existence reflects Mooseville's founding values: you trade for the things you want, never for the things you need.

## 12.2 The Two Laws in Gameplay


The Two Laws — Mechanical Implementation
Law I — Moose Water is free. The Enhancement Works dispenses a minimum of 1 free Moose Water portion per day to the player regardless of save state, story progress, or resource level. This floor cannot be set below 1 by any system. If the Enhancement Works is temporarily degraded (Act 1 story event), the daily minimum of 1 portion still applies via emergency reserves held by Storm Winslow.
Law II — Basic food is free. Marjorie Bell's public meal station always has at least 3 free basic food items available for the player per day. These items restore a small amount of stamina and prevent the player from going hungry. This supply cannot be depleted by the player taking it.
These laws also apply to NPCs within the simulation: the community food supply level affects NPC dialogue flavor (a well-fed town speaks with more contentment). A depleted supply does not affect NPC function — it only affects dialogue tone and provides motivation to restore the supply.

MELVIN — Tier 1 Elder, Town Founder
"As long as the spring glows, we're not done yet."

MARTHA — Tier 1 Elder, Town Co-Founder
"A town is only as strong as the people it refuses to leave behind."
Role: Mother figure, primary strategist of the founding. Now runs the Archive Quarter alongside Gloria Finch's grandchild Clara. The intellectual engine behind most of Mooseville's civic systems.

FERN VALE — Tier 2, Grower (Doris Vale's Grandchild)
"If Grandma Doris could make things grow in rock, I can make them grow in winter."
Role: Primary farming NPC and the player's most enthusiastic early companion. Manages Doris Vale's farm with enormous energy and slightly chaotic organizational skills.
Personality: Young, enthusiastic, obsessed with Moose Water crop variants. Best friend energy — she adopts the player immediately and assumes mutual friendship whether they've earned it or not.
Quest Arc: The Coldroot Project — developing a crop that grows in winter without Moose Water enhancement, making Mooseville food-secure in the harshest season. Requires wilderness foraging for specific soil samples, Cold Shelf mineral finds, and multiple crafting stages. Completion unlocks the Coldroot crop and the greenhouse expansion prerequisite.
Legacy Moment: The first winter Coldroot harvest. Fern and the player pick the crop together at dawn. She names the field after Doris Vale. The field appears on the town map as "Vale's Winter Garden."


REED HARLOW — Tier 2, Builder (Raymond Harlow's Son)
"Stone doesn't lie. Neither do I."
Role: Primary building and construction NPC. Manages all significant structural projects in Mooseville and is the player's key collaborator on home expansions.
Personality: Quiet, methodical, better with materials than with words. He communicates through doing — he shows up when he says he will, works without complaint, and leaves when the job is done. His affection is expressed through making things for people.
Quest Arc: Designing and building the New Arrivals District — permanent housing for incoming moose from the wilderness. Requires the player to source ancient stone from the ruins, specific timber from the Emerald Forest, and river clay from the Marsh Shallows. The district grows visible in the town with each building completed.
Legacy Moment: Reed builds the player a custom piece for the homestead — something specific to the player character's family history — without being asked. He just leaves it by the door one morning with no note.


CLARA FINCH — Tier 2, Archivist (Gloria Finch's Granddaughter)
"The Documents know more than I do. That's why I can't stop reading them."
Role: Primary lore delivery NPC and the player's partner in unraveling the Sacred Documents mystery thread. Manages the Archive Hall with a sense of personal responsibility that sometimes tips into anxiety.
Personality: Brilliant, anxious, carries the weight of history like a physical burden. She knows more about Mooseville's founding than anyone except Melvin and Martha — and she worries constantly that she might misremember something important.
Quest Arc: Translating an untouched section of the Sacred Documents that no one in the Archive has been able to read — a technical manual that, once decoded, reveals specifics about the valley's pre-Starfall survey history. Requires wilderness artifact finds (translated survey markers, fragment documents from the ruins) across Acts 1–2.
Legacy Moment: Clara reads the final translated page aloud to the player at the Archive Hall. The page contains the name of the human who surveyed the valley. A quiet, devastating, beautiful moment.


STORM WINSLOW — Tier 2, Water Watcher (Harold Winslow's Son)
"I know the lake better than I know most people. The lake doesn't change its mind."
Role: Primary water and weather NPC. Monitors the Enhancement Works alongside his civic duties and is the first to notice the system's degradation in Act 1.
Personality: Dramatic, loves weather, secretly terrified that the Enhancement Works is more fragile than the town admits. He performs confidence but is genuinely afraid — and his quest arc is about facing that fear honestly.
Quest Arc: Discovers a new sky-stone seep emerging from the Crystal Lakeshore bed. Needs the player's help securing it before the unstable deposit destabilizes the lake's green-water flow. Requires fishing and diving (swimming obstacle), Spring Knack Tier 1, and Moose Water activation. Resolves the Act 1 Enhancement Works degradation plotline.
Legacy Moment: He admits to the player — quietly, at the lakeshore at dusk — that he's been scared since he was a calf that the water would stop working. "You helped me stop being scared of a thing that might not happen by fixing the thing that was actually happening." He names the lakeshore stabilization site "Winslow's Calm."


JUNE PRICE — Tier 2, Mediator (Evelyn Price's Daughter)
"Everyone has a right to feel at home. Even if they're not sure yet what home means."
Role: Primary new arrivals integration NPC and the player's social compass for community dynamics. Runs the welcome hall in the New Arrivals District and is deeply emotionally attuned.
Personality: Diplomatic, perceptive, first point of contact for all new arrivals. She sees what people need before they can say it. She is endlessly patient with everyone except those who are unkind to newcomers.
Quest Arc: A conflict emerges between two new-arrival groups who have incompatible territorial histories from beyond the valley. June needs the player to mediate — listen to both sides, find a shared need, and propose a compromise. A nuanced, emotionally intelligent quest with no wrong outcome — only more or less graceful ones.
Legacy Moment: The two groups share a meal together. June and the player watch from the doorway. She says: "This is what Mom always said the job was. Not fixing things. Just staying until there's room for everyone."


CHESTER LANG — Tier 1 (surviving First Circle member)
"If it's worth building, it's worth building right. And if it breaks, it was worth building anyway."
Role: Master craftsperson, living link to the First Circle era. Still sharpening tools at dawn. Still repairing whatever breaks. The oldest active working Moosian in the game, and proud of it.
Personality: Old, gruff, and deeply warm beneath three layers of gruffness. He respects effort above all things. He will not help anyone who won't try first. Once the player has demonstrated genuine commitment, he is fiercely loyal.
Quest Arc: His masterwork — building the player's Personal Workshop expansion — but he needs a specific alloy that can only be smelted from a mineral found near the Starfall impact site on the High Shelf. A mid-to-late Act 2 quest that ties the crafting system to the main story geography.
Legacy Moment: He finishes the workshop and stands back to look at it. He says nothing for a long moment. Then: "Melvin built the first workshop with his hooves and a rock and a lot of wrong guesses. Yours is better." He means it as the highest possible compliment.


ATLAS REED — Tier 2, Mapper (Vincent Reed's Son)
"I've mapped every inch of this valley. I'm just not showing you yet."
Role: The exploration gatekeeper NPC — holds the most complete map of the valley and refuses to share it until the player has earned each section through their own exploration.
Personality: Born to explore. Competitive in a playful way. He challenges the player not out of gatekeeping instinct but because he genuinely believes you only understand a place you've found yourself. He is also secretly delighted every time the player names a landmark differently than he did.
Quest Arc: A push-pull relationship of mutual respect: he shares a map section for each biome the player has entered and documented. As the player's map fills in, Atlas becomes more open — first professionally respectful, then genuinely excited that someone else is finally exploring at his level.
Legacy Moment: He shows the player his personal map — hand-drawn, dense with notes, full of landmarks named in his own hand — and asks the player to write their landmark names alongside his. "The valley should have two witnesses." A map image of the completed, dual-authored valley map is permanently available in the journal.

# Section 14 — World Map & Biome Guide


This section is the primary reference for Environment Artists and Level Designers. Every biome has a precise visual identity, mood, and gameplay function. Biomes should feel distinct but connected — as if they are all chapters of the same valley, not separate environments stitched together.

## 14.1 Map Overview

The valley is roughly circular, with Mooseville positioned near the center-south of the playable area. Key spatial relationships:
- Mooseville (Center-South) — the hub; all roads lead back here
- Green Spring (North of town center) — between the homestead and the Emerald Forest; glows visibly from town at night
- Emerald Forest (North, wrapping NW) — the first wilderness area; surrounds the upper valley
- Golden Meadow (West, open) — the valley's breathing room; visible from Mooseville's western edge
- Crystal Lakeshore (Northwest) — a bowl-shaped depression fed by snowmelt; reachable via the meadow path
- Ancient Ruins (East, mid-range) — hidden in the tree line; not visible from Mooseville
- High Shelf (North, elevated) — above the forest canopy; reached by climbing trail
- Sealed Eastern Pass (Northeast, far) — the landslide zone; visible as a dark notch in the mountain ring from the High Shelf
- Far Valley (Beyond the pass, East) — out of sight until Act 3; the unknown

# Section 15 — UI/UX Design


Mooseville's user interface must feel like it was made by Moosians — carved from wood, edged in hammered gold, functional but warm. There is no cold tech HUD. Every interface element should look like it belongs in the world. The UI Artist and UX Designer must treat immersion as the primary constraint, not information density.

## 15.1 UI Philosophy

The guiding aesthetic is: a town that makes its own tools. Every UI element should look like it was hand-made by an intelligent, craft-proud civilization working with wood, stone, pressed paper, and hammered metal. No glass panels. No glowing digital displays. No sharp geometric abstraction. The HUD is warm, minimal, and diegetic in character — it should feel like it grew out of the world, not out of a software toolkit.

## 15.4 Notification System

Mooseville Adventures does not interrupt the player's experience with pop-up notifications during exploration or social interaction. All notifications are queued and delivered through the Journal system:
- A small wood-carved notification panel appears at the bottom of the screen — a gentle tap sound and a warm amber pulse
- The panel shows only: how many notifications are waiting (a carved number)
- Notifications are read by opening the Journal — they become a new auto-written entry
- Quest completions, Closeness level changes, new discoveries, and community events are all delivered this way
- The one exception: the Central Fire event bell rings audibly when a community event begins — an in-world sound, not a UI element

# Section 16 — Audio Direction


Mooseville's audio is not background decoration — it is a primary storytelling medium. The valley should feel like it has a sound, and that sound should evolve as the player's understanding of it deepens. The Composer and Sound Designer are full creative collaborators on this project, not service providers. Their work defines the emotional register of the game as much as the writing does.

## 16.1 Music Philosophy

The Mooseville soundtrack should feel like the valley itself composed it — natural, warm, acoustic-first, with occasional moments of unexpected wonder that earn their emotional weight by contrast with the quieter foundation. No aggressive tracks anywhere in the game. No looping monotony. Music evolves with time of day, player location, and the emotional state of the game's narrative progression.

The Music Rule
If a piece of music could plausibly play in a cozy game set in a modern human town, it is probably right for Mooseville. If it would fit in an action game, an ambient electronic game, or a horror game, it does not belong in Mooseville. When in doubt — quieter, warmer, more acoustic.

## 16.3 Dynamic Music System

- Music transitions smoothly between states using layered stems via Unreal Engine's MetaSound system — no hard cuts between biomes or times of day
- NPC Proximity Layers: Approaching named NPCs at their work locations adds subtle instrument layers. Chester Lang adds a rhythmic hammer-beat. Fern Vale's greenhouse area adds a melodic whistle motif. Storm Winslow's water station area adds a water texture layer.
- Community events have unique music variants that do not play anywhere else in the game — they are reserved exclusively for their event
- The Player's Homestead theme plays only at the homestead — it never bleeds into outdoor areas
- Music parameters: Biome (zone), TimeOfDay (enum), NPCProximity (nearest named NPC ID), NarrativeState (Act 1 / 2 / 3) — all fed into MetaSound parameter system

## 16.5 Sound Design

- Crafting SFX: Every crafting action has a distinct material-based sound — wood chop, stone grind, water pour, metal shape, leaf press. No two crafting sounds are the same.
- Moose Water Audio Identity: A soft resonant hum plus a green shimmer tone — used consistently whenever Moose Water is collected, poured, or applied. This sound is never used for anything else in the game.
- Sky-Stone Deposits: A low resonant heartbeat pulse when the player is within proximity radius. Intensifies as the player approaches. Goes quiet again when the player backs away.
- Footsteps: Distinct layered footstep sounds for: grass, stone paths, wood plank walkways, shallow water, deep water, snow, and mud. Moose hooves on each surface are a satisfying, grounded audio experience.
- NPC Activity Sounds: Named NPCs produce ambient activity sounds at their work locations — Chester's hammer, Fern's trowel, Storm's water measurement tools, the Archive Hall's turning pages.
- Community Fire: The Central Fire has a consistent, warm crackling audio presence. It is the acoustic anchor of Mooseville — always there, always steady.

## Appendix A: Mooseville Glossary


This glossary is the canonical reference for all terminology used in Mooseville Adventures. Writers, designers, and QA must use these terms consistently. Any new term introduced in development must be added here with Game Director and Narrative Designer approval.


Term
Definition
Moosian
Any moose citizen of Mooseville or awakened by contact with the Green Spring or Moose Water. The preferred self-identifier used by all awakened moose.
The Starfall
The meteor event that triggered the awakening, approximately 22 years before game start. The central historical event of Mooseville's founding mythology.
Green Spring
The original spring where sky-stone shards settled after the Starfall. Still glows green at night. Located near the player's homestead. The physical source of the awakening.
Moose Water
Water enhanced by sky-stone contact through the Enhancement Works system. A free civic resource that reverses awakening regression and has multiple gameplay uses. Governed by the First Law.
Mucks
Carved wooden coins; Mooseville's local currency for non-essential goods. Never required for food or Moose Water.
Sky-Stone
Physical fragments of the meteor that fell during the Starfall. The source of the awakening's magical substrate. Found scattered in wilderness deposits throughout the valley.
Sacred Documents
The books, magazines, field guides, and printed manuals found in the old human campsite. Mooseville's founding cultural artifacts. Preserved in the Archive Hall.
The First Circle
Melvin, Martha, and their eight specialist collaborators who founded and shaped Mooseville. The founding generation; their descendants are the primary Tier 2 NPCs.
Enhancement Works
The civic system that channels sky-stone influence into the town's water supply, producing Moose Water for public distribution. A critical infrastructure element with gameplay significance in Act 1.
The Fading Years
The crisis period (Year 4–5 post-Starfall) when the green water stream was diverted, causing regression in some Moosians. Mooseville's defining historical trauma.
The Far Side Arrivals
The first wave of awakened moose who arrived from the opposite side of the valley, brought there temporarily by the diverted green water. Their arrival transformed Mooseville into a welcoming community rather than merely a surviving one.
Knack
The player's progression system. Five Knack Trees (Harvest, Craft, Heart, Trail, Spring) advance through gameplay action rather than XP spending. Each tree has 5 tiers with 3 abilities each.
Trail Stamina
The player's exploration energy resource. Depletes during running, swimming, and climbing. Never causes a fail state — depleted Stamina reduces the player to a walk, never stops them.
Closeness Level
The 1–6 relationship depth scale with each named NPC. Never displayed numerically to the player; expressed through NPC dialogue warmth, availability, and quest depth.
Legacy Moment
The special, unique story beat that occurs at Closeness Level 6 with a Tier 2 NPC (or at story completion for Tier 1 NPCs). Permanently recorded in the Archive Hall. Cannot be repeated or undone.
Archive Hall
The building in the Archive Quarter where Mooseville's history is preserved in writing. The location where the player character's name is formally inscribed at the end of the game — the final narrative reward.
Rendering Bible
The companion technical art document that defines all visual standards for Mooseville Adventures (cel-shading parameters, landscape layer limits, LOD strategy, lighting rules). Authoritative alongside this GDD.
DailyScheduleComponent
The custom UE5 Actor Component that governs NPC daily routines. Subscribes to the TimeManager's DayPhaseChanged event and moves NPCs to their scheduled locations accordingly.
The Two Laws
The two inviolable civic laws of Mooseville: (1) Moose Water is free, (2) Basic food is free. Mechanically enforced in gameplay — the player cannot be denied either resource.

## Appendix B: Design Intention Summary


The Final Word on Mooseville Adventures
Mooseville Adventures is not a game about surviving. It is not a game about conquering. It is not a game about winning.
It is a game about remembering that the best things — the fire that stays lit, the water that stays free, the name that gets carved into stone — are built by people who stayed, who helped, and who looked at the world around them and decided it was worth caring for.
The player carries Melvin and Martha's name. They inherit a town that is already extraordinary. The question the game asks — quietly, warmly, and without judgment — is: what will you add to it?
By the end of the game, they will have an answer. And it will be theirs alone.
That is Mooseville Adventures.
