# Section 1 — Character Art Philosophy

Mooseville Adventures is built on a single promise: every character must make the player feel immediately at home. Not impressed. Not intimidated. Home. The visual language of Mooseville is warm, rounded, approachable, and unmistakably hand-crafted in spirit — as though someone built these characters out of felt, stuffed them with something soft, and sent them out to live their lives in a sun-dappled valley.
### 1A — Design Pillars


Pillar
Description
Practical Application
1 · Readability at Any Distance
Every character must be identifiable at thumbnail size (32×32px). The primary silhouette — shaped by antlers, build, and clothing outline — carries all the recognition work.
Test every new character as a flat black silhouette at 64px width. If you cannot name the character from silhouette alone, redesign the outline.
2 · Silhouette Uniqueness
No two named characters share the same silhouette. Antler shape, body width, clothing bulk, and accessory profiles must all contribute to a unique readable outline per character.
Maintain a silhouette comparison chart (one row per character, flat black fills). Update it whenever a new character is added. No duplicates allowed.
3 · Emotional Expressiveness
Moosians communicate emotion through large eyes, antler angle, ear position, and body lean — not just facial muscles. The entire character is an emotional antenna.
Animators must rig ears and antlers independently. Ear and antler micro-animation is required in all cutscene shots — never static during dialogue.
4 · Warm Color Harmony
Earth tones, emerald greens, and gold accents define the palette. All character colors must harmonize against the four primary environment tones: emerald grass, golden wheat, sky blue, dark forest floor.
Check every new character against the Environment Contrast Test (Section 11). No character should visually "disappear" into any of the four environments.
5 · Family-Friendly Without Being Infantile
Mooseville is appropriate for all ages, but adults must feel respected. NPCs have lives, histories, and emotional complexity. The art should reflect personality and depth, not just cuteness.
Every NPC has at least one visual detail that hints at their backstory (worn leather, ink-stained hooves, notched antlers). These "story details" are mandatory, not optional.

### 1B — Tonal Reference


✦ Creative Direction Quote
"Animal Crossing meets Stardew Valley with a touch of Pixar warmth." Characters must be instantly lovable. They should feel as though they stepped out of an illustrated children's book — but one that adults would keep on their shelf too. Think: the emotional weight of Toy Story characters with the cozy accessibility of Animal Crossing villagers, rendered in the warm-toned palette of a Ghibli countryside.

- Animal Crossing influence: Approachable scale, non-threatening features, consistent world logic, characters with distinct daily routines and personalities.
- Stardew Valley influence: Pixel-adjacent warmth, seasonal color shifts, characters who feel like they were there before the player arrived and will carry on after.
- Pixar influence: Emotionally readable eyes, storytelling through physical design (a worn apron says more than dialogue), visual contrast between generations.
### 1C — Moose Anatomy Philosophy


⚠ Critical — Not Realistic Moose Anatomy
Mooseville characters are stylized to cartoonish extremes. Real moose anatomy is a reference point only, not a blueprint. Artists who have not internalized this distinction should review the Base Proportion Guide (Section 2A) before touching any character work.

Key anatomy departures from real moose (all intentional, all non-negotiable):
- Head size: Enormous relative to body — 1:2.2 head-to-body ratio for adults. Real moose heads are proportionally smaller.
- Snout/nose: Very round, bulbous, takes up the lower 40% of the face. Not elongated like real moose. More like a round rubber ball attached to the face.
- Antlers: Disproportionately huge — should extend well beyond body width. They are the primary visual personality expression tool and should always be compositionally dominant.
- Limbs: Short, thick, rounded — stubby proportions that convey groundedness and warmth. Barely any visible ankle.
- Neck: Essentially absent — shoulders blend directly into head silhouette. No visible neck column.
- Hooves: Small, dark, rounded — a detail, not a feature. Do not elongate or make angular.
- Eyes: Large, wide-set, expressive — the emotional center of every face.

## 2B — Child / Youth Proportions

Youth Moosians appear in two contexts: background ambient children in Mooseville town areas, and child versions of NPCs in memory/flashback sequences. They use a separate proportion standard from adults — more chibi, more cartoonishly compressed, with proportionally enormous heads.

✦ Artist Note — The "Must Read Younger" Rule
Child characters must immediately read as children at any camera distance. If there is any ambiguity, make the head bigger, the limbs shorter, and the antler nubbins smaller. There is no such thing as "too chibi" for Mooseville children. The proportion gap between child and adult must be unmistakable.

# Section 3 — The 7-Layer Clothing Rig System



REF: turn0image1 · Melvin — 7-Layer Clothing Rig Diagram · FIRST-DRAFT DIRECTIONAL — Not Canonical


⚙ Technical Note — Why 7 Layers?
The 7-layer clothing rig system enables real-time outfit swapping in Unreal Engine 5 without re-rigging the base skeleton. Each layer is a separate skeletal mesh component parented to the base character skeleton. Layer visibility and mesh swap operations are handled by the Mooseville Wardrobe Manager system (Blueprint-driven). Artists must deliver each clothing item as a separate mesh per layer — combined meshes are not supported by the Wardrobe Manager and will break runtime swapping.

## 3A — Layer Stack Reference

Layers are numbered back-to-front (Layer 1 is deepest/furthest from camera; Layer 7 is frontmost). In a ¾ view — which is Mooseville's primary gameplay camera angle — the rendering order corresponds directly to perceived depth.

⚙ Technical Note — UE5 Implementation
Skeletal Mesh Components: Each layer is a separate USkeletalMeshComponent on the base ABP_MoosianCharacter Blueprint. Layer components are named SKM_L1 through SKM_L7. Mesh swapping is handled via SetSkeletalMesh() calls from the WardrobeManager subsystem. Color tinting is handled via Material Dynamic Instances — each layer mesh shares the same master material (M_MoosianClothing) with runtime-set scalar and vector parameters for the grayscale mask system (see Section 4). LOD: Each layer mesh must deliver LOD0 (full detail), LOD1 (medium shot), and LOD2 (background/crowd). Layer meshes must be skinned to the same skeleton as the base character body (SK_MoosianAdult or SK_MoosianChild). No separate skeletons per layer.



# Section 4 — The Grayscale Mask Tinting System

The Grayscale Mask Tinting System is the core technical art system powering all color customization in Mooseville Adventures. It enables infinite color variations from a single asset without duplicating meshes, textures, or materials. Every customizable element — hair, clothing, accessories, footwear — uses this pipeline.
## 4A — Hair Tinting System

### Pipeline Overview


Step
Operation
Tool / Location
Notes
Step 1
Create full-color hair texture with all shading, highlights, and curl detail baked in
Substance Painter / Photoshop
Work in full color at this step — shading must be rich and correct at this stage.
Step 2
Desaturate to greyscale — preserving all value relationships (light/shadow/highlight)
Photoshop · Image → Adjustments → Desaturate
Result should be a luminosity-correct greyscale. Verify highlights are near-white (#F8F8F8) and shadow areas are dark grey (#2A2A2A range).
Step 3
Paint red channel mask — solid #FF0000 red over all areas that should be tintable
Photoshop — Red channel mask layer
Tintable areas = all main hair color regions. Leave highlight specular spots as white (#FFFFFF) — these will not be tinted, preserving shine.
Step 4
At runtime, Wardrobe Manager applies chosen PlayerHairColor (Vector3 parameter) to the red-masked areas of the greyscale base
UE5 Material — M_MoosianHair · Parameter: HairTintColor
Multiplicative blend: Greyscale value × HairTintColor = Final pixel color. Preserves all shading detail regardless of chosen color.
Step 5
White areas in mask are ignored by tinting shader — rendered as-is (specular highlights)
Automatic — shader handles masking
Do NOT paint white areas red. Tinted specular highlights look wrong across all hair colors.


✦ Artist Note — Result
One hair mesh + one greyscale texture + one mask texture = infinite color variations with a single material parameter change. No texture duplication, no mesh variants. The system supports any RGB color, meaning artists can introduce new hair colors purely through game design/UX without any art pipeline work after the initial asset is complete.

# Section 5 — Player Character Customization Specification

The player character is an adult Moosian of the founding generation — Melvin and Martha's grown child. They are returning to Mooseville after years away and are customized at game start. Customization can be revisited at the "Looking Glass" mirror in the player's inherited home at any time. The player character must support the full 7-layer clothing system and all tinting channels.

✦ Artist Note — Player Character Design Philosophy
The player character must have a "blank canvas" quality — visually warm and appealing in all default states, but never so distinctive that they feel like an authored character. They are the player's avatar, not a named NPC. Avoid any detail so specific it conflicts with player identity. The default Starter Set B (red polo + navy shorts) was chosen because it is warm, active, and visually neutral — it communicates "ready to start an adventure" without communicating "specific personality."

## 6C — New Arrivals (Wilderness Discoverable)

New Arrivals are Moosians discovered in the wilderness beyond Mooseville's current borders. They awakened from different water sources and have developed in isolation — resulting in slightly wilder appearances, more natural materials, and in some cases unusual fur colorations. They are the same species as Mooseville residents, but their aesthetic reads as "found nature" rather than "built community."

✦ Artist Note — New Arrivals Design Language
New Arrivals should feel like they belong in Mooseville's world, but as if they came from a different chapter of it. Less polished, more elemental. Clothing is woven grass, bark strip wraps, seed-bead decoration, leaf fronds, stone-threaded jewelry. No manufactured clothing. The contrast with Mooseville's tailored garments should be immediately readable.

## 8B — Antler and Ear Body Language


⚙ Technical Note — Independent Antler & Ear Rigging
Ears and antlers MUST be independently rigged from the face and from each other. Each ear has 3 bones (base, mid, tip). Antlers have 2 bones per tine minimum (base + tip) and additional bones at main rack junctions. Micro-animation of ears and antlers is mandatory in all cutscene shots — they must never remain static during any dialogue sequence. Ear and antler movement supplements, reinforces, and sometimes telegraphs facial expression before the face registers it.

# Section 12 — Visual Do's and Don'ts

These rules apply to all character work — new characters, revisions, costume additions, and expression work alike. They encode the fundamental design decisions of Mooseville Adventures. Violations will result in revision requests.

✔ DO  Keep outlines thick and consistent — 3-4px at standard resolution. Moosians are outlined beings. Never feather or fade outlines at any edge. Use warm shadows — all character shadows should have an orange or red-brown tint. Shadow is #3A2010 warm, not cool grey. Ever. Ensure antlers always read in silhouette — antlers must never be visually buried in a busy background. If composition requires it, add a subtle halo/rim light around antler edges. Overlap antlers with backgrounds, not with other character elements. Antlers should frame the character, not clip other NPCs in conversation scenes. Make eyes the emotional core — they should always be the first element the player's eye lands on. Eyes before antlers. Eyes before clothing. Use the golden mane/neck tuft as a warmth anchor — every character who has this tuft should have it slightly golden, regardless of overall color scheme. It's the warm heart of every design. Expand customization imagination — the reference art shows one possibility per element. Propose ten more. The drafts are doors, not walls. Differentiate characters through antler shape, build, and clothing — the three-way combination must create unique silhouettes. Add story-telling details — ink stains, soil patches, worn knees, scratched antler tines. These details make characters feel lived-in. Test every character as a black silhouette at 64px width — if you can't name them, redesign the outline. Keep child proportions clearly distinct from adults — no ambiguity about age reads allowed. Rig ears and antlers independently — micro-animation of both is mandatory in all cutscenes.
✖ DON'T  Don't create realistic moose anatomy — if it could pass as a National Geographic illustration, it's wrong. Push the cartoon further. Don't use cool grey shadows — ever, on any character element. Cool grey shadows are the single most common error in the Mooseville pipeline. They immediately break the warm tonal identity. Don't give two NPCs the same silhouette — at 64px, every character must be uniquely identifiable. No exceptions. Don't use the same fur color for more than 3 named characters — the cast is diverse. Use the full palette. Don't create child characters without clearly childlike proportions — they must read as younger at all times. Ambiguous age creates tonal problems. Don't exactly copy the first-draft reference — the drafts show directional intent, not final design. Always push further. Don't use high-contrast black outlines on dark fur without an edge highlight — dark fur + black outline becomes an undifferentiated dark mass. Add a 1px warm light highlight at the outermost edge of dark fur silhouettes. Don't differentiate character gender through body shape — all Moosians share the same base body type. Only antler color, hair, accessories, and clothing differ. Body shape is neutral and consistent. Don't forget to design clothing backs — the 135° and 180° views get as much screen time as fronts during gameplay. "Forgotten back syndrome" is a revision trigger. Don't make wildlife wear clothing — bears, eagles, deer, and all ambient animals are clearly wildlife by the absence of any manufactured items. Don't deliver combined layer meshes — each of the 7 clothing layers must be a separate mesh. The Wardrobe Manager will break on combined meshes. Don't paint specular highlight areas in the mask channels — specular stays white in both hair and glasses masks, always unmasked.


✦ Final Note — The Spirit of Mooseville
Every character in Mooseville Adventures was built — literally and figuratively — by community. The visual language should always reflect that: warmth over perfection, personality over polish, belonging over spectacle. If a character makes you want to sit with them on a porch and hear their story, you've done your job. If they make you feel slightly uneasy, they need another pass. The bar is simple and the bar is high: every Moosian should feel like a friend you haven't met yet.
