Executive Summary — The Mooseville Rendering Bible is the authoritative reference governing every visual decision made by the technical art and rendering teams during the production of Mooseville. This document defines the studio's hybrid cel-shaded rendering model, the emerald-and-gold visual identity system, shader and material specifications, character and environment rendering rules, VFX and UI standards, naming conventions, pipeline workflow, performance targets, and the do/don’t visual guidelines that enforce the Mooseville aesthetic across all platforms and all disciplines. Rooted in the warm, painterly, storybook sensibility of late-1990s and early-2000s stylized 3D titles — drawing specific inspiration from the rich saturated palettes, soft cel-shaded character work, and inviting natural environments of games like Barbie Horse Adventures — Mooseville builds a distinctive visual identity of its own: a deep emerald forest world alive with golden-hour light, moose-scale architecture, hand-crafted surfaces, and a sense of joyful wonder that must be present in every frame. Every team member who creates, reviews, or integrates visual assets is expected to have read, understood, and internalized this document before contributing to production; deviations from any specification herein require written approval from the Rendering Director.

# 1. Artistic Vision & Rendering Philosophy


Section Intent: Establish the irreducible aesthetic soul of Mooseville so that every downstream technical decision is grounded in shared artistic purpose.

## 1.1 The Mooseville Promise
Every frame of Mooseville must deliver a feeling — not merely a scene. That feeling is warmth, wonder, storybook magic, and lived-in nature. The player should sense that the forest has always been here, that it breathes and grows, that golden light filters through canopy leaves as though the world is perpetually caught in a perfect late-afternoon moment. Characters must feel like they belong to the world — soft, readable, full of personality — and the environment must feel like somewhere a child might have once imagined in the back of a storybook. Nothing should feel cold, clinical, or mechanically generated. Every asset, every shader decision, every color choice must serve this promise.

THE MOOSEVILLE PROMISE
Every frame must answer YES to this question: “Does this feel like stepping into a warm, living, illustrated world full of wonder?” If the answer is no, the frame is not finished.

## 1.2 Core Visual Pillars

Pillar 1 — Emerald Alive
The green world of Mooseville is never static, never flat, never cold. Foliage shifts in the breeze, moss catches dappled light, and the deep emerald palette breathes with warm undertones of gold and amber. Every environment asset must contribute to the sense of a living, photosynthesizing world.


Pillar 2 — Golden Hour Always
Mooseville’s lighting defaults to the warmth and directionality of the golden hour regardless of in-world time of day. Even noon scenes are lit with a slight warm cast; even night scenes carry amber torch-glow as their primary light source. The sun of Mooseville is always generous and inviting, never harsh or bleaching.


Pillar 3 — Storybook Legibility
Every character, prop, and landmark must be immediately readable in silhouette and color at all expected gameplay distances. Cel-shading bands, outlines, and saturated palette choices are all in service of clarity. Mooseville never sacrifices readability for photorealism or complexity.


Pillar 4 — Tactile Warmth
Surfaces in Mooseville feel hand-made. Wood looks carved. Stone looks stacked by careful hands. Fabric looks woven. The material language must communicate craft, care, and age — the sense that someone built this world with love. Roughness values, normal maps, and albedo variation all work together to prevent anything from feeling mass-produced or procedurally cold.


Pillar 5 — Joyful Contrast
Mooseville uses bold, saturated color contrast not for shock but for joy. The interplay of deep emerald forest with warm gold accents, rich bark browns with cream highlights, sky blue with soft lavender — these combinations must feel delightful, balanced, and intentional. Contrast is always in service of clarity and emotion, never gratuitous.

## 1.3 The Barbie Horse Adventures Inspiration
The soft, saturated, hand-painted aesthetic of early 3D equestrian titles — most notably the Barbie Horse Adventures series — serves as one of Mooseville’s primary reference points for emotional tone and visual quality bar. The following specific visual qualities are identified as worth borrowing and adapting:

The world has more mystique and depth than the reference aesthetic, while retaining all of its warmth and accessibility.

## 2.3 Lighting Philosophy — “Golden Hour Default”
Mooseville operates under a core lighting philosophy: the default state of any scene is golden-hour quality light. This means a warm directional key light with a slight downward angle (15°–35° above horizon), a soft warm fill from the opposite direction, and an ambient light that carries at minimum a 3200K warmth. This philosophy applies even at simulated noon (the key light is still warm and directional, not overhead and white) and even at night (the ambient is deep blue-green but characters retain their warm fill light rig).
The reasoning: players spend most of their time in Mooseville’s forests and meadows. These environments are most inviting, most readable, and most emotionally resonant when lit with directional warmth that creates clear shadow definition without harsh contrast. The golden-hour default also means that the emerald palette reads with maximum vibrancy — warm light on cold green produces the characteristic jewel-like quality that is Mooseville’s visual signature.

## 2.6 Forbidden Color Combinations

FORBIDDEN COLOR COMBINATIONS — Never Use
1.  Pure Cyan (#00FFFF) on Pure Magenta (#FF00FF) — Maximally clashing, vibrates, causes visual fatigue; prohibited in all contexts.
2.  Desaturated Grey (#808080) on White (#FFFFFF) — Insufficient contrast, reads as disabled/broken state; use warm cream background instead.
3.  Neon Yellow-Green (#CCFF00) on Emerald Deep (#1B5E20) — Appears clinical and toxic; undermines the organic warmth of the palette.
4.  Pure Black (#000000) on Bark Brown (#5D3A1A) — Insufficient contrast and reads as a texture artifact, not intentional color use.
5.  Cold Blue (#2196F3) on Warm Gold (#FFD700) — Temperature clash destroys the warm-world identity; any blue accent near gold must be softened to sky-blue or lavender.
6.  Saturated Red (#FF0000) on Saturated Green (#00FF00) — Color-blindness failure case; unusable for gameplay-critical information.
7.  White (#FFFFFF) as any surface or background color — Pure white does not exist in Mooseville; always substitute Warm Cream (#FFF8E7) or a tinted near-white.
8.  Pure Black (#000000) as any outline or line color — All outlines must use warm near-black (#1A0F00) or forest black-green (#0D2B0A). See Section 5.

## 2.7 The “Emerald Depth” Rule

RULE: Emerald Depth
All green colors in Mooseville must carry warm undertones — they must lean toward yellow-green on the hue wheel, never toward cyan-green or blue-green. In HSL terms, all environment greens must maintain a hue value between 95° and 130° (yellow-leaning green range). Greens that drift below 95° toward yellow are acceptable with caution; greens that drift above 130° toward cyan are prohibited in all surface albedo, lighting, and UI contexts. When in doubt, add a +5° to +10° hue shift toward yellow and increase saturation slightly — this will restore the signature Mooseville emerald quality.

## 2.8 The “Gold Accent” Rule

RULE: Gold Accent Discipline
Gold (#FFD700 and the warm gold family) is never used as a base fill color for large surfaces. Gold is exclusively reserved for: (1) specular highlights and rim light tints on characters and props, (2) interactable object outlines and UI accents, (3) hero prop surface inlays and decorative embossing, (4) magic VFX endpoint color, (5) text headers in the Rendering Bible and formal documentation. Any surface larger than approximately 0.25m² that appears predominantly gold is a violation of this rule and must be revised to use a warm cream or bark-brown base with gold accent highlights overlaid.


# 3. Shader Rules & Rendering Model


Section Intent: Specify Mooseville’s hybrid rendering model and all shader-level rules in enough technical detail that a rendering engineer can implement or audit any shader against this standard.

## 3.1 Hybrid Rendering Model Overview
Mooseville uses a hybrid rendering model: a cel-shaded diffuse base (the primary aesthetic driver) combined with selective PBR metallic/roughness for specific surface categories where physical plausibility adds to the storybook quality rather than undermining it. The PBR layer is not a compromise — it is a deliberate choice to make water sparkle convincingly, metal gleam warmly, and gem inlays catch light with believable depth. The cel shading is the soul; the PBR details are the craft.
- Cel diffuse base: All surfaces receive a stepped diffuse calculation (2–3 bands depending on surface type). This is the primary lighting evaluation path.
- Selective PBR layer: Applied only to water, metal accents, glass/crystal, and gem inlays. This layer uses standard metallic-roughness inputs and is composited on top of the cel base at a controlled blend weight.
- No ray tracing: Ray tracing is not used in Mooseville’s real-time rendering pipeline. All reflections use baked cube maps or planar reflection passes. All shadows use baked + cascade hybrid.

## 3.4 Shadow Rules
- Characters — NO hard ray-traced shadows. Characters cast only blob/contact shadows (circular Gaussian soft shadow projected onto terrain, radius = character foot radius × 1.2). No cascaded shadow maps from characters onto environment.
- Environment shadows: Baked lightmap shadows for static geometry + real-time cascaded shadow maps (CSM) for dynamic objects and time-of-day variation.
- Maximum CSM cascade count: 3 cascades on PC High; 2 cascades on PC Medium and Console; 1 cascade on Mobile. Far cascade blends into baked lightmap.
- Shadow color: Shadows are never pure black. Shadow color must always be a tinted dark — environment shadows tinted deep teal-green (#0A2010), character contact shadows tinted warm bark brown (#3E2723).
- Shadow softness: PCF (Percentage Closer Filtering) with 4-sample kernel on all platforms minimum; 16-sample on PC High.
## 3.5 Specular Rules
- Character specular: Single-dot Blinn-Phong highlight only. Always tinted warm gold (within 20° hue of #FFD700). Specular power range: 32–64. No anisotropic specular on character skin or fur.
- Environment specular: Disabled on all terrain, foliage, stone, and wood surfaces. Specular enabled ONLY on water (MV_Water) and metal accent surfaces (MV_Metal_Accent).
- Water specular: PBR specular with baked cubemap reflection + planar reflection for hero water bodies. Roughness range 0.05–0.35 (calm to choppy).
- Metal specular: PBR metallic, cubemap-based. Reflection tinted warm gold to match world identity.
## 3.6 Rim Light Specification

CHARACTER RIM LIGHT SPEC — Mandatory
•  Color: Warm amber #FFB347 — never cold-colored, never pure white, never blue or cyan.
•  Width: 0.08–0.12 in screen space (at 1080p = approximately 8–13px wide at closest gameplay distance).
•  Intensity: 0.6–0.8 normalized; rim should complement, not overpower, the diffuse shading.
•  Direction: Opposite to primary key light direction, slightly elevated (10°–20° above horizon opposite key).
•  Applied to: All hero characters (Moose family, named NPCs). Reduced 50% on generic NPC characters. Disabled on props and environment.
•  NEVER USE: Cold rim colors (#4FC3F7 blue, #80DEEA cyan, #B39DDB lavender) on any hero character.

## 3.7 Ambient Occlusion Rules
- Method: SSAO (Screen Space Ambient Occlusion) — enabled on all platforms except Mobile (baked AO only on Mobile).
- Strength range: 0.35–0.55. Below 0.35 reads as absent; above 0.55 creates a “dirty” look inconsistent with the Mooseville aesthetic.
- Radius range: 0.4m–0.8m world-space. Larger radius creates softer, more ambient occlusion suitable for open environments; tighten to 0.4m in dense interior spaces.
- AO color: Never pure black. AO tint must be warm dark green (#0D1A0A) to preserve the emerald identity in shadowed crevices.
- Character AO: Character surfaces use baked AO texture only (packed in texture channel — see Section 12). SSAO is disabled on character layer to prevent “dirty” noise artifacts on moving character surfaces.

## 3.9 Prohibited Shader Practices

PROHIBITED SHADER PRACTICES — Zero Tolerance
1.  No screen-space reflections (SSR) on foliage — SSR on alpha-clipped geometry creates severe artifact bands. Foliage has no reflections.
2.  No subsurface scattering (SSS) on non-organic surfaces — SSS is reserved for primary character skin approximation only. Never on wood, stone, water, or fabric.
3.  No physically-based sky (IBL from HDRI) as sole ambient source — The IBL must always be color-graded to the golden-hour default. Raw HDRI capture from reference photos is never used directly.
4.  No runtime shader compilation — All shader variants must be pre-compiled and stripped at build time. No dynamic shader keyword toggling at runtime.
5.  No normal map on VFX particles — VFX particles use flat color + emissive only. Normal maps on particles waste memory and create visual noise.
6.  No anisotropic specular on character skin — Skin uses single-dot Blinn-Phong only. Anisotropic is reserved for hair/mane (MV_Hair_Anisotropic shader only).
7.  No pure emission on environment architecture — Environment emissive (lanterns, glowing fungi) must use a masked emissive with maximum brightness 2.0 (HDR), not unbounded emission. Prevents bloom overload.
8.  No shadow casting from particle VFX — VFX particles are excluded from shadow casting and receiving in all rendering contexts.

## 4.5 Material Variation Rules

No two adjacent large surfaces (greater than approximately 1m² of visible area) may use the same material instance without at least one variation parameter changed. # 5. Outline / Silhouette System


Section Intent: Define Mooseville’s outline system precisely enough that every element in every scene has a correct, consistent silhouette that reinforces the storybook aesthetic without tipping into cartoon or comic-book territory.

## 5.1 Outline Philosophy
Outlines in Mooseville exist for one purpose: to serve readability. They are not decorative flourishes or aesthetic statements on their own. Their job is to keep characters, props, and foreground elements legible against complex environmental backgrounds — to make the world scannable as a storybook illustration is scannable. Outlines should feel like a natural part of the visual world; at normal gameplay distances, a player should not consciously register that outlines exist. If outlines are noticeable for their own sake, they are too thick, too dark, or too uniform.
## 5.2 Outline Rendering Method
Mooseville uses post-process edge detection based on both depth buffer and normal buffer differentials. This approach (rather than geometry shell / inverse-hull method) is chosen because:
- It scales correctly to all geometry complexity levels without requiring dedicated outline meshes
- It responds to camera distance automatically (combined with distance-based falloff — see 5.5)
- It avoids the z-fighting and backface issues of the geometry shell method on complex meshes
- It can be cleanly disabled per render layer (VFX, UI, skybox) without geometry modification
Edge detection parameters: Depth threshold 0.015 (normalized device coordinates); Normal threshold 0.25 (dot product difference); Combined with luminance edge detection at threshold 0.18 for color-transition edges on flat surfaces.

## 5.4 Color Rules for Outlines

OUTLINE COLOR RULE — Absolute
Pure black (#000000) is prohibited as an outline color in all contexts. All outlines must use warm near-black (#1A0F00) or deep forest green-black (#0D2B0A) as their darkest option. This prevents the “comic strip” appearance that makes outlines feel like a design choice imposed on the world rather than a natural quality of it. The warm undertone in #1A0F00 allows outlines to read as “deep shadow,” not as flat ink lines.

## 5.6 When Outlines Are Disabled
- Cutscene hero close-ups (<1m camera distance): Outlines are disabled on hero character face and head geometry during extreme close-up cutscene shots to prevent thick-line visual distraction during emotional moments.
- Water surfaces: All water geometry is excluded from the outline pass.
- Skybox: Sky dome and cloud geometry are excluded entirely from the outline pass.
- Emissive VFX: Any geometry or particle in the MV_VFX_Additive or MV_VFX_Soft layer receives no outlines — emissive surfaces define their own edges via luminance contrast.
- Foliage beyond LOD1 (>20m): Foliage geometry transitions to impostor billboards at LOD2, and impostors receive no outline rendering. Foliage outlines are also disabled at LOD1 to prevent aliasing on alpha-clipped leaf edges.
## 5.7 Outline Anti-Flicker — Temporal Smoothing
Outlines derived from depth+normal edge detection are susceptible to temporal flicker during character animation, as surface normals change frame-to-frame with skinning. Mooseville’s outline pass must include temporal anti-aliasing (TAA) smoothing of the outline buffer: the edge detection result is accumulated over 4 frames with a 0.25 blend weight per frame (exponential moving average). This eliminates single-frame flicker artifacts caused by animation without blurring the outline at stable camera positions. The TAA accumulation buffer for outlines is separate from the main scene TAA buffer and must be cleared on camera cuts.

# 6. Character Rendering


Section Intent: Define the complete rendering specification for Mooseville characters so that every character is the emotional center of their scene — readable, warm, expressive, and consistent at all distances and lighting conditions.

## 6.1 Character Rendering Overview
Mooseville characters are the emotional heart of the experience. Every rendering decision for characters prioritizes three things: readability at all distances, warmth and approachability at all lighting conditions, and expressiveness that works within the cel-shaded aesthetic. Characters must never feel like they belong to a different visual world than the environment — but within that shared world, they must always win the eye.
## 6.2 Character Shader Layer Stack
The MV_Character shader evaluates in the following order:
- Base Albedo — Flat color from the character’s albedo texture (painted, not photographed). All character albedo is authored at ×1.0 in linear space.
- Cel Diffuse Bands — 3-band cel shading applied via NDotL stepped evaluation. Shadow tint #6B3A1F; midtone = base albedo; highlight = albedo × 1.25, cream-tinted.
- Rim Light — Warm amber (#FFB347) fresnel rim applied after diffuse. Never overdriven; max rim brightness 0.8.
- Specular Dot — Single Blinn-Phong specular dot, gold-tinted, at specular power 32–64. Masked by specular mask texture (eyes, accessories, wet nose — not fur or fabric).
- Emissive (Eyes / Accessories) — Eyes and magical accessories use emissive mask from albedo alpha channel. Emissive color must be warm (no cold emissive on hero characters). Catchlight is baked into eye emissive texture.
- Outline — Applied in post-process after all scene rendering; width and color per Section 5 specification.
## 6.3 Skin / Fur Rendering Rules
- Non-primary characters: No subsurface scattering in any form. Shadow tint is neutral warm (#5A4030). Standard 2-band cel shading.
- Primary characters (Moose family): A light SSS approximation (not true SSS) is achieved by biasing the shadow band color toward warm amber (#6B3A1F) rather than neutral dark. This is not a separate SSS pass — it is achieved entirely through shadow tint color selection in the MV_Character shader. This creates the quality of light “glowing through” the extremities (ears, muzzle, snout area) without the performance cost of real SSS.
- Fur normal maps: Character fur uses a specialized fur-direction normal map that follows the hair flow direction across the body. Normal strength 0.35–0.50 only — never higher, as excess normal intensity creates a “bumpy” look inconsistent with soft storybook fur.
## 6.4 Eye Rendering Specification

EYE RENDERING SPEC — MV_Eye Shader
•  Shader: Dedicated MV_Eye shader — do not use MV_Character on eye geometry.
•  Layer 1: Iris flat color — cel-shaded with 3 bands (dark ring, mid iris, bright iris center). Iris cel bands follow the same pattern as character body but with finer gradation (0.01 smoothstep width).
•  Layer 2: Specular catchlight — always visible, always warm white (#FFFDE7), positioned at the 10 o’clock position (upper-left quadrant of iris). Catchlight is a static texture element baked into the eye emissive, NOT derived from real-time lighting. This ensures characters always look “alive” regardless of scene lighting.
•  Pupil: Soft-edged ellipse with 5% warm dark (#1A0F00) border ring. No hard edge on pupil boundary.
•  Wet rim: Subtle specular on lower eyelid rim (lower waterline) — small Blinn-Phong dot at specular power 128, intensity 0.3.
•  Scale rule: Eyes on Mooseville characters are 15–20% larger than anatomical reference for expressiveness. This is a deliberate design choice and must be preserved through all LOD levels.

## 6.5 Hair / Mane Rendering
- Shader: MV_Hair_Anisotropic — dedicated shader for all hair, mane, and tail surfaces.
- Highlight type: Anisotropic specular highlight oriented along the hair flow direction (tangent-space, derived from UV flow direction). Highlight is a band, not a dot.
- Highlight color: Always warm — within 30° hue of gold (#FFD700). Valid range: #FFB347 (amber) through #FFD700 (gold) through #FFEC8B (light gold). Never use white, silver, or cool-tinted hair highlights.
- Opacity: Hair strands use alpha clip at 0.5. No semi-transparent hair strands. Hair uses sorted transparent rendering only for extreme-close-up cutscene shots (LOD0 only).
- Wind: Hair receives light vertex-color wind animation (amplitude 0.01–0.03 world units, frequency higher than foliage) to simulate natural movement in shared wind system.

## 6.7 Cloth & Accessory Rendering
- Fabric: All character fabric uses MV_Mat_FabricWool — roughness 0.75–0.85, metallic 0.0. Fabric weave pattern baked into normal map. No specular on fabric surfaces.
- Gold accessories: Character gold accessories (belt buckles, antler rings, crowns) use MV_Mat_GoldAccent — metallic 0.90–1.0, roughness 0.15–0.30. Embossed normal map required.
- Shadow casting: Accessories cast no real-time shadows. Their contact with the character body is handled by baked AO in the character’s albedo texture and baked ambient occlusion map.
- LOD simplification: Accessory detail geometry (rivets, buckle details) is removed at LOD2 and beyond. Accessory silhouette must be preserved through LOD2; removed at LOD3 if not visible in silhouette.
## 6.8 Character Lighting Overrides
Characters receive a dedicated fill light rig that is baked into the MV_Character shader, not derived from scene light data. This is implemented as a secondary directional light evaluation in the shader, running separately from the scene light rig:
- Fill light direction: Opposite the key light direction, 15°–25° elevation above horizon.
- Fill light intensity: 15–25% of key light intensity.
- Fill light color: Always warm — minimum color temperature equivalent of 4000K.
- Purpose: Ensures characters never appear silhouetted against bright backgrounds; always retain dimensional form regardless of scene lighting state.
- Override scenario: In cinematics, the character fill light rig intensity may be reduced to 0% to allow cinematic lighting full control — requires explicit flag in the cinematic sequence data.
## 6.9 Facial Animation Rendering
- Blend shape constraint: Blend shape deformation must not distort UV islands. UV coordinates on the face mesh must remain stable within 5% of their rest-pose position at all morph target extremes. Artists must validate UV stability using the UV distortion visualizer before submitting facial blend shapes.
- Morph target normals: Vertex normals on facial blend shapes must be recalculated per-frame using the deformed mesh geometry. Baked rest-pose normals on morph targets are insufficient — they will cause incorrect shading on extreme expressions (surprise, anger, joy).
- Expression shape count: Maximum 52 facial blend shapes per character at LOD0; reduced to 12 at LOD1; 0 (texture-swap only) at LOD2 and beyond.

# 7. Environment Rendering


Section Intent: Ensure that the Mooseville world breathes, lives, and grows — defining all rendering systems that make the environment feel like a place rather than a backdrop.

## 7.1 Environment Rendering Philosophy
The world of Mooseville breathes. Foliage sways, water shimmers, light dapples through the canopy in shifting patterns, and every surface carries the memory of weather and time. The environment must never read as a static backdrop — it is an active, living participant in the story. All environmental rendering decisions must serve this sense of aliveness while remaining within the cel-shaded visual language and performance budgets defined in this bible.
## 7.2 Terrain Rendering
- Splatmap system: Terrain uses a splatmap-based multi-material blending system. Maximum 4 layers per terrain chunk. Layer blending is linear; height-based blending bias is allowed to simulate material accumulation in crevices.
- Normal maps: Terrain normal maps are baked at 4096×4096 per terrain chunk. Runtime normal blending between splatmap layers uses per-layer normal weights.
- Macro variation: Large terrain areas use a distance-based tint overlay — a low-frequency color texture at 1 cycle per 50m world-space to prevent repetitive tiling appearance at aerial camera angles.
- Terrain cel banding: 2-band cel (shadow/lit). Shadow threshold tied to the scene light direction — terrain must respond to time-of-day light direction changes.
- Terrain holes: Terrain holes (for caves, tunnels) use dithered alpha masking. No hard-edge holes. Transition must blend to cave interior within 0.5m.
## 7.3 Foliage Rendering Rules
- Wind animation: All foliage uses vertex-color wind animation. The red channel of vertex color encodes wind influence weight (0 = no movement, 1 = full movement). Amplitude range: 0.02–0.08 world units. Frequency tied to global wind system parameter (shared across all foliage in scene).
- Wind phase offset: Each foliage mesh instance receives a phase offset derived from world position to prevent synchronized movement across all plants simultaneously.
- LOD transition: Foliage beyond 30m uses billboard impostors. Foliage casts no real-time shadows past LOD1 (beyond approximately 8m camera distance).
- Translucency: All broad-leaf foliage uses translucency channel in MV_Mat_LeafBroad. Backlit translucency value 0.25–0.45. Grass and ground cover does not use translucency.
- Alpha cutout: All foliage uses alpha clip (threshold 0.5). No semi-transparent foliage leaves (causes sorting issues). Smoothed alpha clip achieved via mipmapping, not alpha-to-coverage (too noisy at distance).

## 7.5 Water Rendering
All water surfaces use the MV_Water shader with the following specifications:
- Normal scroll — Layer 1: UV scroll rate 0.02 UV units/second, direction aligned to current flow direction. Normal map: tileable water ripple, scale 4.0.
- Normal scroll — Layer 2: UV scroll rate 0.013 UV units/second, direction rotated 35° off Layer 1 (diagonal counter-scroll). Normal map: same source, scale 7.0 (different tiling for variety). Opposite direction sign creates interference pattern.
- Vertex displacement: Maximum 0.3m amplitude sinusoidal displacement. Displacement masked by shoreline proximity (no displacement within 0.5m of shore edge).
- Foam mask: Shore foam rendered via depth-based shoreline detection (scene depth vs. water surface depth). Foam width 0.4m from shore. Foam texture scrolls in flow direction. Foam color warm white (#FFFDE7).
- Depth color blend: Shallow color #6DBFA0 (warm aqua-green), deep color #0D4F3C (deep forest teal). Blend driven by water column depth, fully blended at 2.0m depth.
- Specular/reflection: Planar reflection pass for hero water bodies (rivers, main lake). Cubemap reflection for small puddles and secondary water. No SSAO on water surface.
## 7.6 Sky Rendering
- Procedural sky: Sky uses a procedural sky dome with time-of-day parameters controlling gradient, sun disc, and atmospheric scattering simulation. No static sky cubemaps.
- Color temperature rule: No pure white sky is permitted at any time of day. Minimum sky tint equivalent of 5500K at midday (warmest permissible “neutral” sky). Skies become progressively warmer toward dusk per Section 2.4.
- Sun disc: Visible sun disc uses soft Gaussian bloom halo. Sun disc diameter 2.5° angular. Never pure white — minimum warm cream (#FFF8E7) at highest point.
- Cloud rendering: Clouds use planar billboard cards with soft alpha blend. Cloud color follows sky gradient (lit undersides warm cream; shadowed undersides soft lavender-grey). No volumetric clouds on minimum-spec platforms.
- Stars (night only): Star layer activates below 20% sun elevation. Stars are point sprites, white-warm (#FFF8E7), 50% opacity max to prevent reading as gameplay elements.
## 7.7 Weather Rendering
- Rain: Rain particles use MV_VFX_Soft shader — white-warm streaks, alpha blend, particle lifetime 0.3s, oriented along velocity direction. Screen-space wetness shader modifies surface roughness: -0.35 roughness reduction on wet surfaces. Wetness fades out over 30s after rain ends.
- Fog: Height-based exponential fog. Fog density 0.003–0.008 depending on biome (see table 7.8). Fog color follows ambient temperature — never pure white, never pure grey. Always warm-tinted to biome ambient color.
- Mist/low fog: Ground-level mist uses MV_VFX_Soft particles in low slow-moving sheets. Mist particles: saturation 0.05–0.10 (near-white), opacity 0.15–0.25 per particle, world-space simulation. Used in Emerald Forest biome at dawn.

# 8. VFX Rendering

## 8.1 VFX Philosophy
Effects in Mooseville are magical and organic. They enhance the world without breaking the storybook aesthetic. VFX should feel like natural phenomena elevated to the edge of magic — falling leaves that catch the light a little too perfectly, fireflies that pulse in rhythm with the world’s breath, magical sparkles that feel like concentrated golden-hour light. VFX must never feel hard-edged, techno-industrial, or cold. They must always respect the emerald-and-gold palette and the warm, painterly world they inhabit.

## 8.4 Color Rules for VFX
- Non-magical VFX (smoke, dust, breath, fallen leaves): Use desaturated warm tones. Saturation range 0.05–0.25 (low saturation, warm hue). Examples: warm grey smoke #B0A090, dusty tan #C8B090, pale warm leaf #D4C070.
- Magical VFX: Must use the emerald-to-gold gradient range exclusively. No cool purples, blues, or pinks in standard magic VFX. Special rare events (Berry Purple biome — see Section 2) may use #7B1FA2 as a secondary accent to gold only.
- Additive VFX contrast: Additive-blended particles naturally become brighter against dark backgrounds. All additive VFX must be tested against both dark forest and bright sky backgrounds and must remain aesthetically pleasing in both contexts.
## 8.5 Magic VFX Specification

MAGIC SPARKLE VFX SPEC — MV_VFX_Additive
•  Blend mode: Additive only. Never alpha-blend for sparkle/glow VFX.
•  Color range: #B5E853 (bright magic green) → #FFD700 (gold). Gradient shift over particle lifetime from green to gold.
•  Particle size: 0.02m–0.15m. Size varies with distance from emitter center (smaller at edges, larger at core).
•  Lifetime: 0.4–1.2 seconds. Longer for sustained ambient magic; shorter for impact/burst events.
•  Orientation: Always face camera (billboard — camera-aligned). No world-space oriented sparkles.
•  Texture: Soft star/diamond shape with Gaussian falloff. 4-pointed star preferred over round for legibility at small sizes.
•  Emission curve: Burst at event start (0–0.1s high density), then trailing off with exponential decay.

## 8.6 VFX and Outlines / Decals
- VFX particles are excluded from the outline post-process pass via render layer exclusion. No outline edge detection is applied to VFX geometry.
- MV_VFX_Decal renders on a separate decal render layer with depth test disabled. Decals use multiply or overlay blend against scene color buffer. Decal geometry must be offset 0.001m above the surface it projects onto to prevent z-fighting with surface geometry.
## 8.7 Performance VFX Rules

VFX PERFORMANCE RULE — Mandatory
No VFX effect — including all its emitters, particles, and shader evaluation — may exceed 0.4ms GPU time per frame on minimum-specification hardware. Effects that exceed this budget must be optimized via: (1) reduced particle count, (2) simpler particle texture, (3) LOD-based particle culling, or (4) frame-rate-independent update (GPU particle simulation at half-rate for ambient effects). All VFX must be profiled on minimum-spec hardware before integration. VFX that cannot meet the 0.4ms budget at full quality must have a “Low” quality variant that meets the budget.


# 9. UI Rendering


Section Intent: Define the complete UI rendering standard so that the interface feels like a seamless extension of the Mooseville world — carved wood, gold leaf, and warm legibility — rather than a disconnected overlay.

## 9.1 UI Rendering Philosophy
Mooseville’s UI is an extension of the world — it should feel carved from wood and inlaid with gold leaf, not like a separate HUD bolted onto the game. Every UI element should feel as though a master craftsperson of Mooseville made it: panels have grain, borders have weight, interactive elements glint with warm gold, and typography is warm, readable, and never harsh. The UI must not feel digital, cold, or generic.
## 9.2 UI Shader
- Shader: All UI elements use MV_UI_Flat — an unlit, depth-test-disabled shader that always renders on top of the scene.
- No lighting: No scene lighting, no shadows, no cel shading on UI elements. UI is self-contained and self-lit.
- 9-slice scaling: All bordered UI panels (inventory slots, dialog boxes, HUD frames) use 9-slice scaling to preserve corner and border quality at all target resolutions. Non-9-sliced scaling of bordered UI panels is prohibited.
- Render order: UI renders after all post-processing is applied to the scene. UI does not receive bloom, tone mapping, color grading, or vignette — those effects apply to the scene layer only.

## 9.4 UI Outline Specification
- Interactive elements (buttons, slots, selectable items): 2.5px warm gold outline (#FFD700), opacity 100%. This outline pulses at 0.5Hz (alternating 100%→70% opacity) when the element is focused/hovered.
- Text over dark backgrounds: 1.5px warm white (#FFF8E7) outline at 60% opacity on display text (>20px) appearing over complex backgrounds. Body text (<20px) relies on background panel rather than text outline.
- Decorative/passive panels: 0px outline. Panel boundaries are defined by background fill contrast, not outlines.
- Disabled elements: 1.5px outline at 30% opacity, desaturated gold (#D4C080). Never remove the outline from disabled interactive elements — the muted outline communicates disabled state while preserving layout.
## 9.5 UI Typography Rendering
- Font: Calibri (body text, labels), Candara (display headers, dialog speaker names). Both fonts from the ClearType Collection — no external fonts.
- Anti-aliasing mode: Subpixel anti-aliasing for body text (10–16pt); smooth (grey-scale) AA for display text (>18pt) and text rendered at non-native scale.
- Hinting: Grid-fitted hinting for body text to preserve pixel alignment and legibility at small sizes; auto-hinting for display text.
- Minimum legible size at 1080p: 14px for body text, 20px for headers and dialog text, 10px absolute minimum for any text element. Text below 14px must use a simplified text style (no outline, maximum contrast background).
- Line height: 1.4× for body text, 1.2× for headers. No text elements with line-height below 1.2.
## 9.6 Icon Rendering Specification
- Art resolution: All icons are authored at 2× their display resolution. A 64×64 display icon is authored at 128×128 and downsampled. This ensures clean sub-pixel details at display size.
- Drop shadow: All icons use a drop shadow: offset 2px at 45° (down-right), blur radius 2px, opacity 0.35, color warm near-black (#1A0F00). Shadow is baked into the icon texture — not applied as a runtime effect.
- Color rule: No icon uses pure black (#000000) or pure white (#FFFFFF) as primary colors. Darkest icon color: #1A0F00. Lightest icon color: #FFF8E7 (warm cream).
- Icon outline: All icons have a 1.5px warm near-black internal outline baked into the art to preserve legibility against varying background panels.
- Icon atlas: Icons are packed into sprite atlases of 2048×2048. Maximum 64 icons per atlas. Minimum 4px padding between icons in atlas.
## 9.7 Inventory and Map Rendering
- Inventory: The inventory grid background uses a tiling wood-panel texture (MV_Mat_WoodOld style, at 1 tile per 80px at 1080p). Individual item slots use a lighter wood tone with embossed border. Item slot size minimum 64×64px at 1080p.
- Map: Map overlay uses a parchment-shader panel (MV_UI_Flat with parchment albedo #FFF8E7, slight warm vignette at edges simulating page curl and edge burn). Map edge uses an edge-burn effect — darkening to #8D6E63 at the outer 10% of the map panel. Map text uses Cambria italic at 12px minimum.
- Map icons: All map icons rendered at 2× art resolution. Map uses its own icon atlas separate from gameplay UI icons.
## 9.8 Screen-Space Effects During Gameplay
- Vignette: Subtle vignette — opacity 0.12, color warm brown (#5D3A1A), feathered from 70% screen radius to edge. Applied to scene layer only; not to UI layer.
- No chromatic aberration: Chromatic aberration is prohibited in Mooseville. It conflicts with the clean, storybook aesthetic and causes visual discomfort.
- No film grain: Film grain is prohibited. Mooseville is a clean, illustrated world — grain reads as technical artifact, not stylistic choice.
- No motion blur on UI: The UI layer never receives motion blur. Scene layer motion blur is disabled during standard gameplay (enabled only in specific cinematic sequences where approved by rendering director).
- Damage/impact flash: Damage feedback uses a brief full-screen tint (red #C62828, opacity 0.18, duration 0.25s ease-out). This tint applies to scene layer only — UI remains unaffected.
## 9.9 Accessibility
- All UI text must achieve a minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio (WCAG AA) against its immediate background at all times.
- Interactive elements must have a minimum 44×44px touch target regardless of visual size. Touch targets may be larger than the visible element.
- Critical gameplay information (health, timer) must never rely on color alone — always paired with icon, text, or shape indicator to support color-blind players.
- All interactive elements must be distinguishable by shape and position in addition to color.
- UI must support a High Contrast Mode that increases all text outlines to 2.5px and reduces decorative texture opacity to 30% on all panels.

# 10. Scene Composition & Camera Rules

Every scene in Mooseville is framed as a painting. The rule of thirds is always active. Hero subjects are positioned at power points (intersections of the thirds grid). Depth is created through foreground, midground, and background layering. The eye should be led naturally through the frame from a foreground anchor, through a midground subject, to a background point of interest. No scene should feel accidental in its composition — every gameplay camera position, every cinematic angle, every environmental staging should feel curated.

## 10.3 Depth of Field Rules
- Gameplay: DoF is never active during gameplay. Player control must never be accompanied by bokeh blur — this creates confusion about what is and is not interactable.
- Cinematics only: DoF is enabled only during cutscenes and cinematic sequences.
- Bokeh shape: Circular bokeh. No hexagonal or polygonal aperture shapes — they feel mechanical and undermine the organic world.

- Focus distance control: Cinematic DoF focus distance animated via spline curve in the cutscene sequence. Never instant focus shifts — minimum 0.5s ease between focus distances.

## 10.5 Bloom Specification
- Threshold: 0.85 (HDR luminance). Only surfaces brighter than 0.85 HDR contribute to bloom.
- Intensity: 0.4 — conservative; bloom should enhance without dominating. Mooseville’s world glows gently, not aggressively.
- Scatter: 0.7 — wide bloom scatter for soft, painterly glow rather than tight halos.
- Maximum bloom luminance: Bloom contribution must never exceed 15% of total frame luminance. If exceeded, reduce emissive brightness on offending sources.
- Bloom color tint: Warm — bloom inherits source color. No white bloom override. No cold-tinted bloom.
## 10.6 Tone Mapping
- Method: ACES filmic tone mapping. This is the Mooseville standard and must not be substituted with Reinhard, GT, or custom tone mapping operators without explicit Rendering Director approval.
- Black point: 0.01 — no absolute blacks. Shadows retain warmth and detail.
- White point: 1.0 (standard ACES white). Do not push white point below 0.9 (creates grey, washed-out look).
- Black crush rule: No scene element may be crushed below 3% luminance after tone mapping. If shadows appear too dark, add warm ambient fill rather than adjusting the tone mapping curve.
## 10.7 Color Grading LUT
- LUT Resolution: 32×32×32 (industry standard). Higher resolution LUTs are accepted but not required.
- Midtone grade: +5° hue rotation toward yellow-orange (warm midtones). This is the primary driver of Mooseville’s “golden” feel.
- Shadow grade: +3° hue rotation toward deep green (shadows have forest green undertone, not grey or blue). This preserves the emerald identity in shadow areas.
- Highlight grade: Highlights preserved near neutral — minimal grade on highlights to prevent overwarming bright surfaces. Specular highlights should retain their source color accurately.
- Saturation: +5% overall saturation boost applied in LUT. Do not pre-saturate textures to compensate — LUT saturation handles world-wide saturation enrichment.
## 10.8 Cinematic Framing Rules
- Letterbox aspect ratio: 2.35:1 for all cinematic and cutscene sequences. Letterbox bars rendered warm dark brown (#1A0F00), not pure black.
- Action safe zone: All animated content must remain within the 90% action safe zone.
- Title safe zone: All text, subtitles, and UI indicators must remain within the 85% title safe zone.
- Shot composition: All cutscene shots must include a composition review pass. Reference the rule of thirds, leading lines, and Mooseville’s visual pillars when framing each shot.
## 10.9 Forbidden Composition Elements

FORBIDDEN COMPOSITION PRACTICES
1.  Perfectly centered symmetrical framing for dramatic moments — Centered symmetry reads as comedy or parody; dramatic and emotional beats must use off-center compositions.
2.  Floating UI during cutscenes — All HUD UI elements must be hidden during cutscenes. No health bars, quest markers, or icons visible during cinematic sequences.
3.  Camera clipping through geometry — No camera position may pass through any collision mesh. All camera paths in cutscenes must be validated for geometry intersection.
4.  Empty sky as primary composition element — The sky should be background, not subject. Frames that are >60% sky without a strong foreground or character subject are considered unframed and must be revised.
5.  Camera shake during ambient gameplay — Camera shake is restricted to specific high-impact gameplay events (large landing, explosion). Ambient idle camera shake is prohibited.
6.  Horizon tilt beyond ±5° during gameplay — Dutch angle is a cinematic tool only. Gameplay camera must maintain near-horizontal horizon at all times for spatial clarity.

## 11.2 LOD Transition Rules
- Transition method: LOD crossfade using dither dissolve — the outgoing LOD dithers out while the incoming LOD dithers in over a 0.5m camera-movement crossfade distance. Hard LOD pop transitions are never acceptable in any shipped content.
- LOD0 → LOD1: At 8m camera distance.
- LOD1 → LOD2: At 20m camera distance.
- LOD2 → LOD3: At 45m camera distance.
- LOD3 → Impostor: At 80m camera distance (applicable to trees, large props, and background architecture only).
- LOD bias by platform: Console/Mobile applies a −1 LOD bias (objects transition to lower LODs at shorter distances). PC High applies +0 bias (standard distances).
## 11.3 Impostor / Billboard Rules
- All impostors baked from the LOD0 mesh at 8 rotation angles (every 45°) × 4 elevation steps (0°, 15°, 30°, 45°).
- Impostor atlas resolution: 1024×1024 per tree species. Multiple species may share a 2048×2048 atlas with 4 species per atlas at 512×512 each.
- Impostor textures include: Albedo, Normal (approximate), and Alpha mask. No roughness or metallic on impostors.
- Impostor normal is derived from the LOD2 simplified mesh normal bake, not the LOD0 mesh — this prevents excessively sharp normal features on billboards.
- Impostors must be regenerated whenever the source mesh LOD0 changes shape significantly (more than 10% vertex displacement in any region).
## 11.4 Occlusion Culling
- Method: Engine-native occlusion culling (Umbra-based or equivalent). Mandatory for all platforms.
- Minimum occluder size: 2m³ — objects smaller than this do not participate as occluders (computational cost exceeds benefit). Characters are not occluders.
- Maximum portal count per scene zone: 32 portals. Each biome zone is treated as one scene zone. Zones are separated by portal planes at transition points.
- Occlusion latency: 1-frame occlusion latency is acceptable (objects may be visible for 1 frame before occlusion culling activates). 2-frame latency maximum on Mobile.
## 11.5 Batching Strategy
- Static batching: Applied to environment props with fewer than 500 triangles that share the same material instance. Maximum 64 meshes per static batch.
- GPU instancing: Mandatory for all foliage (grass, ferns, flowers) and repeating rocks. Instancing data submitted via compute-buffer per frame. All instanced objects must share the same material instance (variation via per-instance properties: tint, roughness offset).
- Dynamic batching: Disabled. Mooseville uses SRP Batcher (or equivalent render pipeline batcher) instead. Dynamic batching is incompatible with per-instance material properties needed for variation rules.

## 11.7 Memory Streaming Rules
- Texture streaming: All textures above 256×256 are streamed. Streaming budget per zone: 512MB on PC High, 256MB on PC Medium, 128MB on Console/Mobile.

## 11.8 Shader Variant Rules
- Maximum 4 shader keyword permutations per shader. Keyword combinations must be evaluated — 4 keywords at 2 states each = 16 variants maximum. All 16 must be acceptable.
- Unused shader variants are stripped at build time using the shader variant stripping tool. No variant stripping may be skipped in production builds.
- No runtime shader compilation. All shaders compile at load time for the specific scene or at first-launch shader warm-up. Mid-session shader compilation causes hitches and is prohibited in shipped builds.
- Shader warm-up list must be maintained by the tech art team and reviewed before each platform submission.
## 11.9 Profiling Checkpoints — Mandatory Gates

MANDATORY PROFILING GATES
1.  Pre-Alpha Art Lock: First-pass performance profile on all target platforms. Identify all assets currently over budget. No new content created until over-budget assets are resolved.
2.  Alpha Content Freeze: Full profiling run on all biomes simultaneously loaded. Validate draw call budgets, LOD transitions, and memory streaming. All assets must meet LOD spec.
3.  Beta Content Freeze: Complete platform performance test. All post-processing effects enabled. VFX particle budgets validated at maximum simulated density. No regressions from Alpha gate.
4.  Pre-Final Optimization Pass: Dedicated 2-week optimization sprint. Shader variant stripping. Texture compression verification. Impostor atlas regeneration for any changed meshes.
5.  Gold Candidate Submission: Final platform certification performance test. Must maintain target FPS for minimum 30 consecutive minutes on a full playthrough sequence per platform. Zero frame-rate drops below target −10% threshold.
6.  Post-Launch Patch Gate: Any patch that includes new art assets must pass a lightweight profiling gate (5-minute profile run, draw call count check, memory check) before submission.

## 12.2 Albedo Guidelines
- No pure white: Maximum albedo channel value is 240/255 (0.94 normalized). Pure white washes out under bloom and tone mapping, losing all surface detail.
- No pure black: Minimum albedo channel value is 15/255 (0.059 normalized). Pure black prevents any ambient light read-back, creating dead, flat surface areas.
- Linear color space: All albedo authored in linear color space. sRGB conversion happens on import to the engine. Never author albedo in gamma-corrected color space — this causes double-gamma and washed-out colors.
- Maximum saturation (environment albedo): Maximum saturation for environment surface albedo is 210/255 on any single channel (roughly 82% HSV saturation). Character albedo may reach up to 240/255 on primary color channels for costume accents.
## 12.3 Normal Map Convention
- Convention: OpenGL normal map convention (Y-up). Green channel = up (towards light). Red channel = right.
- Authoring resolution: All normal maps authored at 2× their final shipped resolution. A 1024×1024 shipped normal is authored and baked at 2048×2048, then downsampled on export.
- Channel verification: Green channel = up must be visually verified on every normal map before submission. A simple lit sphere preview confirms correct orientation. Inverted green channels are a common error that causes light to appear to come from underground.
- Downsampling filter: Normal maps must use a tangent-space-aware downsampling filter (normalize after downsampling) to preserve correct normal vector lengths in mip levels.

## 12.5 Texture Tiling Rules
- No texture may tile visibly at normal gameplay camera distances (within 15m player view distance).
- Maximum visible tile frequency: once per 4m in world space. A 2×2m tile pattern on a 10m wall is a violation.
- Large surfaces (>8m in any dimension) must use macro variation overlays: a secondary low-frequency color/detail texture layered at 1 cycle per 20–50m to break tiling perception.
- Tiling verification: All environment textures must pass a tiling check in-engine at 15m player distance before submission. The “squint test” (reviewing the scene with eyes slightly unfocused) is an effective tiling detection method.
## 12.6 UV Mapping Rules
- Lightmapped objects: No UV overlaps on any object that receives baked lighting. UV island overlap causes incorrect lightmap baking.
- Unique surface UVs: Hero props and architectural elements use unique (non-tiling) UV layout in 0–1 UV space. Allows unique texture detail and decal projection.
- Tileable material UVs: Terrain and large repeating surfaces may use tiling UVs. Tiling UVs must respect the 4m-per-tile maximum frequency rule.
- UV seam placement: UV seams must be placed in areas of low visual prominence — undersides, back faces, crevices. No UV seams on forward-facing principal surfaces.
- UV density consistency: Maintain consistent texel density across assets of the same category. Reference density: environment props at 512 texels per meter, characters at 1024 texels per meter at LOD0.
## 12.7 Mip Map Rules
- Full mip chain: Always generate the complete mip chain down to 1×1 for all textures. Partial mip chains cause rendering artifacts at distance and are never acceptable.
- No sharpen filter on mips: Sharpening during mip generation creates aliasing halos at distance. Use box filter (smooth surfaces) or Kaiser filter (detail/normal maps) only.
- Mip level 0 enforcement: When profiling, disable mip streaming and verify that mip level 0 (highest resolution) is never needed at gameplay camera distances beyond 5m for environment surfaces. If LOD0 textures are being streamed at 15m, the mip bias is incorrect.

# 14. Pipeline Rules & Workflow Standards


Section Intent: Define the production pipeline from digital content creation through QA and integration, ensuring every asset that enters the engine is correct, optimized, and production-ready.

## 14.1 Art-to-Engine Pipeline Overview
The Mooseville art pipeline follows a linear validation model: no asset progresses to the next stage until it passes all checks at the current stage. The pipeline stages are:
- DCC Authoring — Asset created in Blender, Maya, ZBrush, Substance Painter, or approved DCC tool.
- Pre-Export Validation — Artist validates polygon count, UV layout, naming, pivot, scale, and mesh cleanliness.
- Export (FBX / PNG) — Asset exported per export standards (Section 14.2). No automated export scripts bypassing validation.
- Import Validation (Engine) — Automated import validation checklist runs on ingest (Section 14.3). Assets failing validation are flagged and returned to artist.
- Material Assignment — Tech artist assigns engine material instances from approved master materials. No default materials on any asset.
- LOD Generation — LOD meshes generated (manual preferred; auto-LOD reviewed and approved by tech art lead before use).
- QA Review — Rendering QA checklist (Section 14.7) completed by designated QA reviewer.
- Integration — Asset placed in scene by environment artist or tech artist. Scene profiling validated after integration.

## 14.4 Material Assignment Rules
- No default materials: No asset may be integrated into a scene with a default, engine-generated, or placeholder material applied to any surface. All materials must be engine material instances of an approved master material (MV_Shd_ family).
- Material instance hierarchy: Material instances must derive from the correct master material. Characters use MV_Shd_Character as their root. Creating material instances that reimplement master shader logic is prohibited.
- Material assignment review: All material assignments on hero assets (main characters, hero props) must be reviewed by the tech art lead before integration sign-off.
- One material per LOD level: Each LOD level may use a simplified or different material variant from the higher LOD. The lower-LOD material must visually match the higher-LOD material at the crossfade distance.
## 14.5 Lighting Bake Standards
- Lightmap UV resolution: Hero architecture — 1 lightmap texel per 4cm surface area at 1024×1024 lightmap. Background architecture — 1 texel per 8cm. Small props — 1 texel per 6cm.
- Bake time limits: Single-zone full lightmap bake must complete within 4 hours on the reference bake workstation. Bakes exceeding 4 hours must be optimized (split into sub-zones or reduce lightmap resolution).
- Lightmap atlas padding: Minimum 4px padding between UV islands at 1024 resolution (8px at 2048, 2px at 512). Insufficient padding causes lightmap bleed artifacts at edges.
- Bake validation: All lightmap bakes must be reviewed in the engine at full scene scale after baking. Common artifacts to check: lightmap seams, over-darkened crevices (over-baked AO), light bleed at padded edges.
## 14.6 Version Control Rules
- All art assets committed to version control (Git with LFS or Perforce) with a descriptive commit message including: asset name, change description, and issue/task reference number.
- Binary files (.fbx, .png, .dds, .psd) must use LFS (Large File Storage). Binary files not tracked with LFS will be rejected on push.
- No single file may exceed 50MB without written approval from the tech art lead. Files over 100MB are prohibited without rendering director approval.
- Texture source files (.psd, .spp, .zpr) are committed to /Source/ only, not to /Art/. The /Art/ folder contains only production-ready flat exports.
- No art asset may be committed to a shared branch during active lightmap baking. Baking must be done on a dedicated bake branch and merged after completion.
## 14.7 QA Rendering Checklist — 10-Point Visual QA

QA VISUAL RENDERING CHECKLIST — All 10 Must Pass
1.  No Z-fighting: No two surfaces produce Z-fighting (flickering) at any camera distance or angle. Verified by moving camera slowly past asset.
2.  Correct LOD transitions: LOD crossfade is smooth (dither dissolve) with no pop at any transition distance. Verified by walking toward and away from asset at all LOD crossfade distances.
3.  Outline correct: Outline width, color, and opacity match Section 5 specification for this asset type. No flicker during animation.
4.  Materials match spec: All materials are correct instances of approved master materials. No default/placeholder materials present. Material visual result matches specification in this bible.
5.  No visible UV seams at gameplay distance: UV seams are not visible when viewed from 3m to 15m camera distance under standard scene lighting.
6.  Correct shadow behavior: Characters produce only contact blob shadows. Environment receives correct cascade shadows. No hard ray-traced shadows on characters.
7.  Correct cel shading band count: Asset uses the correct number of cel bands per its category (3 for hero characters, 2 for environment, 1 for VFX). Verified in standard scene lighting.
8.  No texture tiling artifacts: No visible tiling patterns on any surface at standard gameplay camera distance (15m). Macro variation overlays active on large surfaces.
9.  Bloom and emissive within spec: No emissive surface produces bloom exceeding 15% of frame luminance. Emissive maximum HDR 2.0 verified.
10.  Performance within budget: Asset integration does not push scene above draw call or triangle budget for the target platform. Verified in the in-engine profiler.

## 15.2 Visual Anti-Patterns
The following eight failure modes are the most common ways that Mooseville assets fail to meet the visual quality bar. Each is described with how to spot it in-engine and how to fix it.

Anti-Pattern 1 — “The Cold Forest”
Description: Environment greens have shifted toward cyan or blue-green, making the forest feel clinical, alien, or cold rather than warm and organic.
How to Spot: Compare any green surface against the reference swatch #388E3C. If the in-engine result reads as noticeably cooler/more teal than the swatch, it has shifted. Also detectable by adding the LUT color grade — cold greens will look distinctly different from warm greens under the golden grade.
How to Fix: Apply a +10° hue shift toward yellow in the albedo texture source. Verify the material’s tint parameter has no cool bias applied. Check that the biome ambient color is not overcooling the scene (Section 7.8).


Anti-Pattern 2 — “Comic Strip”
Description: Outlines are too thick, too pure-black, or too uniform, making the world look like a printed comic book rather than a painted storybook.
How to Spot: If outlines are immediately visible to the eye as a design feature (rather than a subtle clarifying edge), they are too thick or too dark. Check: is the outline color a pure #000000? Is the width above 2.0px for non-UI elements?
How to Fix: Reduce outline width to spec (Section 5.3). Replace pure black with warm near-black #1A0F00. Verify distance falloff is functioning (outlines at 20m should be nearly invisible).


Anti-Pattern 3 — “Dirty World”
Description: SSAO is too strong, making shadowed crevices read as visually dirty, grimy, or aged beyond the intended weathering tier.
How to Spot: Surface joints, window edges, and rock crevices appear unnaturally dark — darker than any cast shadow in the scene. The world feels grubby rather than warm and lived-in.
How to Fix: Reduce SSAO strength to the 0.35–0.55 range. Verify AO tint is warm dark green (#0A1F0A) not neutral black. Check that character surfaces have SSAO disabled on their layer.


Anti-Pattern 4 — “Plastic Characters”
Description: Characters appear shiny, plastic, or overly smooth — typically caused by excessive specular highlight intensity, incorrect specular color (cool-tinted instead of gold-tinted), or missing normal map variation.
How to Spot: Character surfaces catch hard bright specular highlights visible from multiple camera angles simultaneously. The specular is white or cool-white rather than warm gold.
How to Fix: Reduce specular power below 64. Tint specular color toward warm gold within 20° hue. Verify specular mask texture is correctly isolating specular to intended surfaces (nose, eyes, accessories — not fur or fabric).


Anti-Pattern 5 — “Overblown World”
Description: Bloom is excessive — emissive surfaces, lanterns, and magic VFX are producing bloom halos so large they obscure surrounding geometry and degrade readability.
How to Spot: Any emissive source where the bloom radius exceeds 10% of screen height. Look for emissive lanterns where the bloom is larger than the lantern itself.
How to Fix: Reduce emissive HDR brightness to maximum 2.0. Set bloom intensity to 0.4 per spec. Check bloom threshold is at 0.85 — if below 0.7, non-emissive surfaces will contribute to bloom. Verify no single VFX source is producing unmasked unbounded emission.


Anti-Pattern 6 — “Tiling Carpet”
Description: Environment surface textures are visibly tiling — the eye picks up a repeating grid pattern across terrain, walls, or large floor surfaces.
How to Spot: Stand 10–15m from any large surface and look for the tell-tale grid of repeating detail. The “squint test” (eyes slightly unfocused) reveals tiling patterns that the eye filters out at sharp focus.
How to Fix: Add a macro variation overlay texture at 1 cycle per 20–50m. Vary the UV scale between adjacent material instances (×1.25 or ×0.8 variation). If tiling is from a single texture, introduce a second tiling layer at a different scale and blend subtly.


Anti-Pattern 7 — “Flat Gold”
Description: Gold accent surfaces appear flat, plastic, or uniform — lacking the embossed pattern, subtle roughness variation, and depth that makes Mooseville’s gold feel like hammered and crafted metal.
How to Spot: Gold surfaces that appear uniform in specular response at all viewing angles. No visible surface detail in the normal map. Gold reading as a uniform yellow blob rather than a warm, textured metallic material.
How to Fix: Apply embossed pattern normal map at 0.6–0.8 strength (herringbone, floral, or geometric pattern). Add subtle roughness variation (0.05–0.10 variation across surface). Verify metallic value is 0.9–1.0 and PBR specular is evaluating correctly.


Anti-Pattern 8 — “Disconnected HUD”
Description: The UI feels like a separate technical overlay bolted onto the game rather than a natural extension of the Mooseville world — typically caused by cold colors, geometric sans-serif UI, or elements that don’t use the warm wood-and-gold material language.
How to Spot: The UI uses cool blues or neutral whites as primary panel colors. Panel backgrounds are flat grey or pure white. Interactive elements don’t use the gold outline system. Typography uses a non-approved cold font.
How to Fix: Apply wood-panel texture to all inventory and dialog panel backgrounds. Replace cool colors with UI color system (Section 9.3). Verify gold outline (#FFD700) is applied to all interactive elements. Confirm all fonts are from the ClearType Collection (Calibri, Candara).

Pass / Fail Marker
1
Does the frame feel warm — does light carry a directional golden-hour quality, even if the scene is set at night or midday?
YES / NO
2
Are all green surfaces warm (yellow-leaning), without any cold cyan or blue-green shifts?
YES / NO
3
Are characters clearly readable in silhouette and cel shading against the environment background at the current camera distance?
YES / NO
4
Do outlines feel like a natural part of the world, not a visible design choice imposed on it? (If the player would consciously notice outlines, they are too strong.)
YES / NO
5
Is the bloom subtle and enhancing — lanterns and emissives glow gently without overwhelming surrounding geometry?
YES / NO
6
Does the UI feel like it belongs to the Mooseville world — wood, gold, warm cream — rather than a generic digital overlay?
YES / NO
7
Are VFX effects within the emerald-to-gold palette (for magic) or desaturated warm (for natural effects)? No cold or clinical VFX colors?
YES / NO
8
Is ambient occlusion subtle and warm — adding depth without making any surface appear dirty, grimy, or over-darkened?
YES / NO
9
Are all shadow values warm-tinted (no pure black shadows or neutral grey shadow areas) and legible against their surface?
YES / NO
10
Does the composition feel like a painting — with clear foreground, midground, background, and a subject positioned at a power point?
YES / NO
11
Is the water (if present) alive — scrolling normals visible, shoreline foam present, depth color blend from aqua to deep teal correct?
YES / NO
12
Is foliage (if present) animated — gently swaying with per-instance phase offset, backlit by translucency where sunlit?
YES / NO
13
Are gold accent surfaces textured, embossed, and small — used as highlights and details, never as large fill surfaces?
YES / NO
14
Does the frame meet performance targets for its target platform — draw call budget respected, FPS stable at target, no frame hitches?
YES / NO
15
If you were to describe this frame in one sentence, would that sentence naturally include the words: warm, living, storybook, wonder? Does the frame embody the Mooseville Promise?
YES / NO


THE FINAL STANDARD
A frame of Mooseville that answers YES to all 15 quality bar questions is a frame we are proud to ship. A frame that answers NO to even one is a frame that needs more work. The quality bar is not optional, not aspirational — it is the minimum standard for every pixel we deliver to players.
