Character Art Philosophy

Critical Framing — Reference Art is Directional Only: All shared reference art throughout this document is FIRST-DRAFT DIRECTIONAL ONLY — not canonical. Artists must treat every piece of reference as a starting point, not a finish line. Colors, clothing, hairstyles, accessories, proportions, and silhouettes must all be massively expanded beyond what the drafts show. Do not replicate the drafts literally — use them to understand the emotional direction, then push further. The goal is to feel like Mooseville, not to photocopy it.

1A — Design Pillars

# Pillar Description Testing / Enforcement
1 Readability at Any Distance Every character must be identifiable at thumbnail size (32×32 px). The primary silhouette shaped by antlers, build, and clothing outline carries all recognition work. Test every new character as a flat black silhouette at 64 px width. If you cannot name the character from silhouette alone, redesign the outline.
2 Silhouette Uniqueness No two named characters share the same silhouette. Antler shape, body width, clothing bulk, and accessory profiles must all contribute to a unique readable outline per character. Maintain a silhouette comparison chart (one row per character, flat black fills). No duplicates allowed.
3 Emotional Expressiveness Moosians communicate emotion through large eyes, antler angle, ear position, and body lean — not just facial muscles. The entire character is an emotional antenna. Animators must rig ears and antlers independently. Ear and antler micro-animation is required in all cutscene shots — never static during dialogue.
4 Warm Color Harmony Earth tones, emerald greens, and gold accents define the palette. All character colors must harmonize against the four primary environment tones: emerald grass, golden wheat, sky blue, dark forest floor. Check every new character against the Environment Contrast Test (Section 11). No character should visually "disappear" into any of the four environments.
5 Family-Friendly Without Being Infantile Mooseville is appropriate for all ages, but adults must feel respected. NPCs have lives, histories, and emotional complexity. Every NPC has at least one visual detail that hints at their backstory (worn leather, ink-stained hooves, notched antlers). These "story details" are mandatory, not optional.

1B — Tonal Reference

"Animal Crossing meets Stardew Valley with a touch of Pixar warmth." Characters must be instantly lovable. They should feel as though they stepped out of an illustrated children's book — but one that adults would keep on their shelf too. Think: the emotional weight of Toy Story characters with the cozy accessibility of Animal Crossing villagers, rendered in the warm-toned palette of a Ghibli countryside.
🍃

Animal Crossing Influence

  • Approachable scale, non-threatening features
  • Consistent world logic throughout
  • Characters with distinct daily routines and personalities
🌾

Stardew Valley Influence

  • Pixel-adjacent warmth in color and form
  • Seasonal color shifts reflected in character palettes
  • Characters who feel like they were there before the player arrived
🎬

Pixar Influence

  • Emotionally readable eyes at every distance
  • Storytelling through physical design (a worn apron says more than dialogue)
  • Visual contrast between generations of characters

1C — Moose Anatomy Philosophy

CRITICAL — Not Realistic Moose Anatomy: Mooseville characters are stylized to cartoonish extremes. Real moose anatomy is a reference point only, not a blueprint.
📏

Key Anatomy Departures — All Intentional, All Non-Negotiable

  • Head size: Enormous relative to body — 1:2.2 head-to-body ratio for adults
  • Snout/nose: Very round, bulbous, takes up the lower 40% of the face. Not elongated like real moose — more like a round rubber ball attached to the face
  • Antlers: Disproportionately huge — should extend well beyond body width. Primary visual personality expression tool, always compositionally dominant
  • Limbs: Short, thick, rounded — stubby proportions that convey groundedness and warmth. Barely any visible ankle
  • Neck: Essentially absent — shoulders blend directly into head silhouette. No visible neck column
  • Hooves: Small, dark, rounded — a detail, not a feature
  • Eyes: Large, wide-set, expressive — the emotional center of every face
📐

Base Moose Anatomy Reference

All measurements use Head Units (H) — the height of the character's head from chin to top of skull, not including antlers.

2A — Adult Proportions

Body Region Measurement Design Notes
Total body height (no antlers) 3.2 H Head accounts for ~1H of total height. Compact, grounded, not tall and lanky.
Head width 1.1 H (slightly wider than tall) Round and wide. Never tall and narrow.
Snout protrusion from face 0.3 H forward Very round, bulbous, occupies lower 40% of face height. Dark warm brown (#3D1C02 range).
Antler height above skull 0.8–1.2 H (varies by style) Antlers should always extend beyond shoulder width. They are a visual CROWN.
Antler span (widest point) 1.6–2.4 H wide (varies by style) The wider the antlers, the more space/authority the character occupies.
Torso height 1.2 H Round barrel shape. No visible neck — shoulders blur into base of skull. No waist definition.
Torso width 1.0–1.3 H (varies by character bulk) Raymond Harlow 1.3H wide. Evelyn Price 0.95H wide. Width = visual weight.
Arm length 0.9 H from shoulder to hoof tip Short and thick. Slightly curved downward. Hooves small and rounded at tips.
Arm width 0.25 H at widest Thick, no defined elbow protrusion. Gentle taper from shoulder to hoof.
Leg length 0.8 H from hip to hoof Very short. Characters should look slightly "planted" — close to the ground by design.
Leg width 0.22 H at thigh, 0.18 H at ankle Slight widening at ankle/hoof junction. Subtle grey-taupe hoof coloration on lower ankle.
Hoof size 0.12 H tall, 0.15 H wide Small, rounded, dark charcoal (#2A2A2A). Subtle gloss highlight spot on top surface.
Eye diameter 0.22 H Large white sclera, single dark iris with white highlight dot. Eyes placed wide apart on face.
Eye placement (horizontal) 0.55 H apart (center to center) Wide-set. Eyes sit in upper-middle face zone, above snout centerline.
Ear width / height 0.3 H wide, 0.2 H tall Wide, flat, slightly droopy in resting position. Salmon/peach inner color. Key emotion indicator.
Ear placement Side of head at midline, below antler bases Ears and antlers occupy the same "crown zone."
Neck tuft/mane 0.25 H tall, 0.4 H wide Fluffy golden-yellow fur at chin/neck junction. Present on most characters. Warmth anchor.

2B — Child/Youth Proportions

Youth Moosians appear as background ambient children in town areas and as child versions of NPCs in memory/flashback sequences.
The "Must Read Younger" Rule: Child characters must immediately read as children at any camera distance. If there is any ambiguity, make the head bigger, the limbs shorter, and the antler nubbins smaller. There is no such thing as "too chibi" for Mooseville children.
Body Region Adult Child Change / Notes
Total body height (no antlers) 3.2 H 2.5 H ~60% of adult height in world units.
Head-to-body ratio 1:2.2 1:1.6 Head is much larger relative to body. Most recognizable chibi indicator.
Head % of total height ~30% ~40% Critical for readability as "young."
Torso shape Rounded barrel Egg-shaped, even rounder Barely differentiated from the head visually.
Leg length 0.8 H 0.4 H Very short — barely visible below torso hem.
Arm length 0.9 H 0.5 H Tiny, rounded nubs. Gesture range is limited and endearing.
Eye diameter relative to face 0.22 H (20% of face width) 0.28 H (25% of face width) Proportionally even larger eyes.
Antler nubbins height 0.8–1.2 H (full rack) 0.08–0.12 H (tiny nubbins) 10–15% of adult size. Soft, rounded tips. Do NOT give children sharp tines.
Mane/neck tuft 0.25 H 0.35 H (proportionally larger) Oversized tuft on small frame — one of the most endearing visual qualities.
Clothing layers Up to 7 Maximum 3 (Layers 1, 3, 5 only) Simplified layering. Children's silhouettes should read clean and uncomplicated.
Expression complexity 8 nuanced expressions 6 binary expressions Big Happy, Big Sad, Surprised, Confused, Scared, Determined. Less emotional subtlety by design.

The 7-Layer Clothing Rig System

Why 7 Layers: The 7-layer clothing rig system enables real-time outfit swapping in Unreal Engine 5 without re-rigging the base skeleton. Each layer is a separate skeletal mesh component parented to the base character skeleton. Layer visibility and mesh swap operations are handled by the Mooseville Wardrobe Manager system (Blueprint-driven). Artists must deliver each clothing item as a separate mesh per layer — combined meshes are not supported.
L1
Braces / Collar — Innermost Undergarment
Everything that peeks out from under the main shirt. Undershirt collars, brace straps, camisoles, turtlenecks, bandanas.
Notable: Martha's yellow undershirt collar
Partial — collar/strap tips only visible at neckline
L2
Arm_1 (Back Arm) — Rendered Behind Torso
The "back" arm — typically the left arm in ¾ view. Any arm sleeve belonging to the back arm: shirt sleeve, jacket sleeve, bare fur arm, arm band.
Notable: Melvin's flannel sleeve (back arm)
Partially occluded by torso in ¾ view
L3
Dress Top / Shirt Body — Primary Clothing Silhouette
Main upper body garment. Defines the character's visual identity most. Polo shirts, blouses, dresses (upper portion), jacket bodies, overalls bib panel, tunics, hoodies.
Notable: Melvin's teal overalls bib panel · Martha's red floral dress body
Always fully visible
L4
Bust / Torso Overlay — Front Chest Accessories
Front chest accessories ON TOP of the main shirt body. Often contains the character's most recognizable visual signature. Suspender clips, chest pockets, badges, bow ties, apron bib front, embroidered patches, medals.
Notable: Melvin's gold wheat sheaf emblem (unique identifier) · Chester's leather apron front
Fully visible. Critical for character identification at distance
L5
Leg_1 (Back Leg) — Rendered Behind Front Leg
Back leg, rendered behind the front leg. Trouser back leg, skirt back panel, shorts back, overall leg (back).
Notable: Melvin's teal overall leg (back)
Partially occluded by front leg in ¾ view
L6
Leg_2 (Front Leg + Footwear) — Most Screen Time
Front leg — the visible leg in ¾ view. Also includes footwear at hoof level. Gets the most "screen time" during walking animations. Trouser front leg, skirt front panel, shorts front, footwear.
Notable: Player's red sneakers · Vincent's muddy expedition boots · Chester's prosthetic wooden hoof tips
Fully visible
L7
Arm_2 (Front Arm) — Primary Gesture & Action Arm
The "front" arm — typically the right arm in ¾ view. Rendered in front of ALL other layers. Primary gesturing, tool-holding, and emoting arm. Front arm sleeve, held items (basket, tool, book, map), wrist accessories.
Notable: Pell Harlow's measuring cord bracelet · Lotte Bell's market tray · Vincent's compass tool
Always fully visible. The "action arm."
⚙️

UE5 Implementation Notes

  • Each layer is a separate USkeletalMeshComponent on ABP_MoosianCharacter Blueprint
  • Layer components named SKM_L1 through SKM_L7
  • Mesh swapping handled via SetSkeletalMesh() calls from WardrobeManager subsystem
  • Color tinting via Material Dynamic Instances — each layer shares M_MoosianClothing master material
  • Runtime-set scalar and vector parameters handle tinting
  • LOD: Each layer mesh must deliver LOD0, LOD1, and LOD2
  • All meshes must be skinned to SK_MoosianAdult or SK_MoosianChild
  • No separate skeletons per layer — share the base skeleton

Grayscale Mask Tinting System

The Grayscale Mask Tinting System is the core technical art system powering all color customization. It enables infinite color variations from a single asset without duplicating meshes, textures, or materials.

4A — Hair Tinting System Pipeline

  1. Create full-color hair texture with all shading, highlights, and curl detail baked in (Substance Painter / Photoshop). Work in full color — shading must be rich and correct.
  2. Desaturate to greyscale — preserving all value relationships (light/shadow/highlight). Photoshop: Image → Adjustments → Desaturate. Result should be luminosity-correct greyscale. Verify highlights are near-white (#F8F8F8) and shadow areas are dark grey (#2A2A2A range).
  3. Paint red channel mask — solid #FF0000 red over all areas that should be tintable. (Photoshop — Red channel mask layer)
  4. Store greyscale as RGB channel + red mask in Alpha. The shader reads: Greyscale luminosity × TintColor → final tinted hair. This multiplies the tint color against the greyscale value, preserving all shading relationships with any chosen color.
The greyscale file is your lighting bake. Once correct in greyscale, it will be correct in ANY tint color. A poorly shaded greyscale cannot be fixed by tint color selection — fix the greyscale first.

4B — Glasses Dual-Channel System

Glasses use a two-region tint mask:

Region A — Frame: Red channel mask (#FF0000). Tinted by FrameTintColor parameter.

Example: Gloria Finch's frames — warm tortoiseshell range (#8B4513 → #C4762A). Renna Finch's frames — same design, cooler modern tortoiseshell.
Region B — Lenses: Green channel mask (#00FF00). Tinted by LensTintColor parameter.

Lens tint should default to near-transparent (#E8F0FF, very low opacity). Never fully opaque lenses.

4C — Other Maskable Accessories

Accessory TypeMask ChannelNotes
Scarves / Bandanas Red channel = full fabric region Full fabric area is tintable
Belts / Straps Red channel = leather areas Leather areas only in red channel
Gold Accents (buckles, antler rings) Dedicated material — not greyscale Use MV_Mat_GoldAccent PBR gold directly. See Rendering Bible Section 4. Not greyscale-tinted.

Expression Sheet Standards

All named NPCs require all 8 expressions at LOD0. These are minimum required deliverables — additional nuanced expressions are encouraged but not required.

8A — Required 8 Expressions

1
Neutral / Resting
Ears at half-droop. Antlers slightly forward. Eyes at rest. Soft closed mouth.
The "schedule walking" expression.
2
Warm Greeting
Eyes slightly wider, soft smile, ears perked upward at 15°, antlers slightly elevated (happy resting tension).
Closeness Level 1–2 expression when player approaches.
3
Engaged / Interested
Head tilted 10–15°, one ear forward, eyes wider, brow (skin above eye) slightly raised.
"Tell me more" expression.
4
Thoughtful / Considering
Eyes down and slightly closed, one ear back, slight head nod downward.
Thinking pose — decision moments.
5
Concerned / Worried
Brow drawn down, ears back and lower, eyes slightly wider.
Quest reveal moments and story tension beats.

Visual Do's & Don'ts

✓ Do

  • Push antlers to be disproportionately large — they are the emotional crown, not just anatomy
  • Maintain soil/mud/ink stains as persistent character texture overlays where specified
  • Make every NPC immediately readable from their back silhouette alone
  • Make clothing "lived in" — Weathering Tier 2 is the default for all NPC owned garments
  • Ensure eyes are always the brightest, most readable element on the face
  • Vary torso width across characters — visual weight variation creates social hierarchy at a glance
  • Test every character against the Environment Contrast Test (Section 11)
  • Maintain gold wheat sheaf emblem on Melvin at ALL times in ALL costumes

✕ Don't

  • Give any two named NPCs the same silhouette — check the silhouette chart
  • Use cool grey tones in character shadows — all cel shadows must use warm tints
  • Use pure black (#000000) in any character texture — use warm near-black (#1A0F00) range
  • Add visible neck columns — the neck is absent by design; shoulders merge into head
  • Make any character's eyes look anxious or fearful as a default state — Mooseville is warm
  • Use the same antler style for two named NPCs of the same generation
  • Stylize wildlife characters with Moosian proportions — they remain naturalistic
  • Use fantasy or non-moose fur colors for player character or named NPCs
Every character in Mooseville should look like they have been here for years — wearing their story on their fur, their clothing, their antlers. If a character looks "fresh off the assembly line," go back and add the details that time would have left behind.
